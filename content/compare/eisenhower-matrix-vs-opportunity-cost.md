## Eisenhower Matrix

Separate what's urgent from what's important. Most people spend their lives on the wrong quadrant.

[Read the full model →](/models/eisenhower-matrix/)

## Opportunity Cost

The true cost of anything is whatever you give up to get it — including the next best alternative.

[Read the full model →](/models/opportunity-cost/)

## What's the difference?

The Eisenhower Matrix categorises tasks by urgency and importance to help you prioritise. Opportunity Cost asks what you give up by choosing one option over another. The Matrix helps you decide what to work on. Opportunity Cost helps you understand the true price of that choice.

## When to use each

Use the Eisenhower Matrix at the start of each week to sort your tasks and protect time for important-but-not-urgent work. Use Opportunity Cost thinking when evaluating any significant commitment — what's the best alternative use of the time, money, or energy you're about to spend?

## In practice

Eisenhower himself used ruthless prioritisation to plan D-Day, focusing only on decisions that required his personal judgment. Netflix understood Opportunity Cost better than Blockbuster — recognising that customers were giving up time, convenience, and anxiety to the late-fee model.

## They work together

These models aren't competitors — they're complements. The best thinkers use both, selecting the right tool for the right situation. Eisenhower Matrix and Opportunity Cost often appear together in [Model Chains](/learn/model-chains/), where the output of one feeds into the other.
