## Loss Aversion

Losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good — and this asymmetry distorts nearly every decision you make.

[Read the full model →](/models/loss-aversion/)

## Status Quo Bias

We prefer the current state of affairs simply because it's familiar — even when alternatives are objectively better.

[Read the full model →](/models/status-quo-bias/)

## What's the difference?

Loss Aversion is the psychological tendency to feel losses roughly twice as strongly as equivalent gains. Status Quo Bias is the preference for the current state of affairs. They're related but distinct: Loss Aversion explains why change feels threatening (potential loss outweighs potential gain). Status Quo Bias describes the resulting behaviour (defaulting to the current state).

## When to use each

Use Loss Aversion awareness when evaluating any change — ask whether the potential loss is genuinely worse than the potential gain, or whether your brain is simply weighting losses more heavily. Use Status Quo Bias awareness when you notice yourself choosing to keep things as they are without active evaluation.

## In practice

Kodak's executives exhibited both: Loss Aversion made the potential loss of the film business feel more threatening than the potential gain of leading digital photography. Status Quo Bias meant they defaulted to protecting the existing business rather than actively choosing between the two paths.

## They work together

These models aren't competitors — they're complements. The best thinkers use both, selecting the right tool for the right situation. Loss Aversion and Status Quo Bias often appear together in [Model Chains](/learn/model-chains/), where the output of one feeds into the other.
