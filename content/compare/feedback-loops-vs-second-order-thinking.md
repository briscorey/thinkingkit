## Feedback Loops

Every system has outputs that feed back into inputs — reinforcing or balancing the system's behaviour over time.

[Read the full model →](/models/feedback-loops/)

## Second-Order Thinking

Consider not just the immediate consequences of a decision, but the consequences of those consequences.

[Read the full model →](/models/second-order-thinking/)

## What's the difference?

Feedback Loops describe the structural mechanism by which systems amplify or stabilise change over time. Second-Order Thinking is a mental practice of tracing consequences beyond the obvious first effect. Feedback Loops are about system structure. Second-Order Thinking is about cognitive discipline.

## When to use each

Use Feedback Loops to understand why a system is behaving the way it does — look for the reinforcing or balancing loops driving the behaviour. Use Second-Order Thinking before making a decision — trace the chain of consequences to anticipate what happens after the obvious first result.

## In practice

The Three Mile Island disaster resulted from a feedback loop failure — faulty instruments turned a negative feedback loop into a positive one. Rent control policies illustrate Second-Order Thinking — the first-order effect (lower rents) leads to second-order effects (reduced housing supply) that worsen the original problem.

## They work together

These models aren't competitors — they're complements. The best thinkers use both, selecting the right tool for the right situation. Feedback Loops and Second-Order Thinking often appear together in [Model Chains](/learn/model-chains/), where the output of one feeds into the other.
