## Confirmation Bias

We instinctively seek out information that confirms what we already believe — and ignore what contradicts it.

[Read the full model →](/models/confirmation-bias/)

## Sunk Cost Fallacy

Money, time, or effort already spent should not influence future decisions — but it almost always does.

[Read the full model →](/models/sunk-cost-fallacy/)

## What's the difference?

Confirmation Bias makes you seek evidence that supports what you already believe. The Sunk Cost Fallacy makes you continue investing in something because of what you've already spent. Both keep you locked into bad positions, but through different mechanisms — one distorts your evidence-gathering, the other distorts your cost-benefit analysis.

## When to use each

Watch for Confirmation Bias when you're forming or evaluating beliefs. Watch for the Sunk Cost Fallacy when you're deciding whether to continue or quit something you've already invested in.

## In practice

The CIA's failure to predict the Soviet collapse was driven by Confirmation Bias — analysts filtered evidence through their existing threat model. The Concorde flew for 27 unprofitable years because of the Sunk Cost Fallacy — neither Britain nor France could admit the billions already spent were gone.

These models aren't competitors — they're complements. The best thinkers use both, selecting the right tool for the right situation. Confirmation Bias and Sunk Cost Fallacy often appear together in [Model Chains](/learn/model-chains/), where the output of one feeds into the other.
