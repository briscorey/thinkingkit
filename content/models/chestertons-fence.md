## How it works

Before removing something, understand why it was put there in the first place.

This model helps you recognise situations where this pattern is at play and adjust your thinking accordingly. Understanding it does not make you immune, but it gives you a framework for catching it in action and making better decisions as a result.

## Case study: How the removal of wolves nearly destroyed Yellowstone

In the early 1900s, the U.S. government eliminated wolves from Yellowstone National Park. Wolves killed livestock and seemed to serve no useful purpose. The "fence" — the wolf population — was removed without understanding why it existed in the ecosystem.

Without wolves, elk populations exploded. The elk overgrazed riverbank vegetation. Without vegetation, riverbanks eroded. Rivers widened and became shallower. Beaver populations collapsed because they needed the willows that elk had eaten. Fish populations declined in the degraded waterways. Songbird populations dropped because their nesting habitat disappeared.

When wolves were reintroduced in 1995, the cascade reversed. Elk moved away from riverbanks. Vegetation regrew. Rivers narrowed and deepened. Beavers returned. Fish rebounded. The wolves didn't just control elk — they stabilised an entire ecosystem. The "useless" fence had been load-bearing.

## When to use it

Apply this model whenever you notice its pattern appearing in your decision-making, your team's behaviour, or the systems around you. It is especially valuable in high-stakes situations where the cost of this error is significant.

## Try it now

Think of a recent decision or situation where this model might have been relevant. How would your approach have changed if you had explicitly considered it?
