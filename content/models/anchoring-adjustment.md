## How it works

Kahneman and Tversky demonstrated that people make estimates by starting from an initial value (the anchor) and adjusting from it — but the adjustment is almost always insufficient. The anchor disproportionately influences the final estimate, even when it's completely arbitrary.

In one famous experiment, participants spun a rigged wheel that landed on either 10 or 65, then estimated the percentage of African countries in the United Nations. Those who saw 10 estimated 25%. Those who saw 65 estimated 45%. A random number on a wheel shifted their estimates of a factual question by 20 percentage points.

## Case study: How real estate agents are anchored by listing prices they know are wrong

Even experienced real estate agents — professionals who price properties for a living — are significantly influenced by arbitrary listing prices. In a study by Northcraft and Neale, agents were given identical property information but different listing prices. Agents who saw a higher listing price appraised the property 12% higher than agents who saw a lower listing price.

When told about the anchoring effect, agents denied being influenced. They believed their appraisals were based on objective property analysis. The anchor operated below conscious awareness, even in trained professionals making judgments in their area of expertise.

## When to use it

Apply anchoring awareness in any estimation or negotiation. Ask: "What was the first number I encountered? Is my estimate drifting toward that number?" In negotiations, recognise that making the first offer sets the anchor.

## Try it now

Next time you need to estimate something, write down your estimate before looking at anyone else's number. Then compare. If your estimate shifts significantly after seeing theirs, anchoring is at work.
