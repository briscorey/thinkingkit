## How it works

The same information, delivered identically, lands completely differently depending on who receives it. Effective communication starts with understanding the listener — their knowledge, motivations, concerns, and decision-making context — before crafting the message.

Herbert Clark's "audience design" framework shows that skilled communicators continuously model their listener's mental state and adapt in real time: what do they already know? What jargon will they understand? What do they care about? What will make them act?

## Case study: How Churchill's wartime speeches used different language for different audiences

Winston Churchill addressed Parliament, the British public, and the American Congress with substantively similar messages — but radically different language, tone, and framing. To Parliament: precise, legal, policy-oriented. To the British public via radio: emotional, defiant, using simple Anglo-Saxon words. To Congress: flattering, emphasising shared values, appealing to American self-interest.

The content was consistent. The audience design was masterful. Each audience received the message in the form most likely to produce the desired response. Churchill didn't have three different messages — he had one message with three different audience-appropriate deliveries.

## When to use it

Apply audience design before any communication that matters. Ask: "Who is receiving this? What do they already know? What do they care about? What do I want them to do after receiving this?"

## Try it now

Take a message you need to send. Before writing it, write three bullet points about your audience: what they know, what they care about, and what action you want them to take. Now draft the message. Does it address all three?
