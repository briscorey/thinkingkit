## How it works

George Akerlof showed that when buyers can't verify quality before purchase, high-quality sellers withdraw from the market because they can't get fair prices. Buyers, knowing this, lower their willingness to pay — driving out more quality sellers. The market degrades until only "lemons" remain.

The solution requires quality signals that are costly or difficult to fake: warranties, certifications, brand reputation, return policies, third-party reviews. These mechanisms reduce information asymmetry and allow quality sellers to differentiate themselves.

## Case study: How eBay solved the lemons problem with a reputation system

When eBay launched in 1995, it faced a classic lemons problem: buyers couldn't inspect products before purchasing from anonymous sellers. Why would anyone trust a stranger to ship a genuine product after receiving payment?

eBay's reputation system — buyer and seller reviews, star ratings, and feedback scores — created a quality signal that was costly to fake (you needed many successful transactions to build a high rating). Sellers with strong reputations could charge premium prices. The reputation system didn't eliminate fraud, but it reduced information asymmetry enough to make the market functional. Without it, eBay would have been a market for lemons.

## When to use it

Look for lemon markets whenever quality is hard for buyers to verify. Ask: "What mechanisms exist to signal quality? Are they working, or is the market degrading because good sellers can't distinguish themselves from bad ones?"

## Try it now

Think of a market you participate in — as buyer or seller. Can quality be verified before purchase? If not, what reputation, warranty, or certification mechanisms exist? Are they sufficient?
