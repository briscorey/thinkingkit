## How it works

Status quo bias is the preference for the current state of affairs. People tend to treat the existing situation as a baseline and view any change as a potential loss. This is closely linked to loss aversion — the psychological tendency to weigh losses more heavily than equivalent gains.

The practical effect: we stay in jobs longer than we should, keep subscriptions we don't use, hold investments past their optimal point, and resist organisational changes that would objectively improve things.

## Real-world examples

**Organ donation.** Countries with opt-out organ donation systems (where you're a donor unless you actively choose not to be) have donation rates above 90%. Countries with opt-in systems typically have rates below 20%. Same population, same decision — but the status quo is different.

**Career.** People stay in mediocre jobs for years because the discomfort of change feels larger than the discomfort of stagnation. The current situation is the "zero point," and any move away from it feels like risk.

## When to use it

Test for status quo bias whenever you're choosing to keep things as they are. Ask: "If I were starting fresh, with no history, would I choose this current situation?" If the answer is no, status quo bias may be holding you in place.

## Common mistakes

The mistake is assuming all stability is bias. Sometimes the status quo really is the best option. The point is to check whether you're staying because it's genuinely optimal, or because change feels threatening.

## Try it now

Identify one area of your life where you've maintained the default for over a year — a subscription, a routine, a job, a relationship pattern. Ask: "If I could design this from scratch right now, would I design it exactly this way?" If not, what's actually stopping you from changing?
