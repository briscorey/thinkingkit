## How it works

Robert Zajonc discovered that simply being exposed to something repeatedly makes us like it more — even when we're not consciously aware of the exposure. This applies to faces, words, melodies, brands, ideas, and foods. Familiarity breeds not contempt, but preference.

The effect is powerful because it operates unconsciously. You don't decide to like something more because you've seen it before. Your brain simply processes familiar stimuli more fluently, and it misinterprets that processing ease as genuine preference.

## Real-world examples

**Advertising.** Brand advertising doesn't need to persuade you of anything specific. It just needs to make you familiar with the brand. When you later face a choice, the familiar option feels safer and more trustworthy.

**Music.** Songs you initially dislike often grow on you with repeated listening. Radio stations exploit this — songs get played repeatedly until familiarity creates preference.

## When to use it

Apply mere exposure awareness whenever you're evaluating options and one feels "right" for no clear reason. Ask: "Am I drawn to this because it's genuinely better, or because it's more familiar?" This is especially important in hiring (preferring candidates who remind you of yourself), investing (preferring companies you've heard of), and adopting new tools or methods.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is concluding that familiarity always misleads. Sometimes the familiar option really is better — experience with something can produce genuine, justified preference. The key is distinguishing between familiarity-as-evidence and familiarity-as-bias.

## Try it now

Think of a preference you hold strongly — a brand, a tool, a method. How much of that preference comes from genuine evaluation, and how much from the simple fact that you've used it for years?
