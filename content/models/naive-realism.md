## How it works

Naive realism is the belief that you perceive reality objectively and without bias, and therefore anyone who disagrees with you must be uninformed, irrational, or biased. It's the mother of all cognitive biases because it makes you blind to all your other biases.

The belief has three tenets. First: "I see the world as it really is." Second: "If others had access to the same information, they'd agree with me." Third: "If they still disagree, they must be biased, stupid, or acting in bad faith."

This makes genuine disagreement almost impossible, because each side believes their perception is objective reality and the other side is distorted.

## Case study: How the Milgram experiment revealed that normal people authorise evil

In 1961, Stanley Milgram conducted his famous obedience experiments at Yale. Participants believed they were administering increasingly powerful electric shocks to another person (actually an actor). 65% of participants administered the maximum 450-volt shock, despite hearing screams and pleas to stop.

The most revealing aspect wasn't the obedience itself — it was the prediction. Before the experiment, Milgram asked 40 psychiatrists to estimate how many participants would administer the maximum shock. They predicted 1 in 1,000. They were off by a factor of 650.

This massive prediction error is naive realism at work. The psychiatrists assessed the situation from their own perspective — "I would never shock someone" — and assumed others would see it the same way. They couldn't imagine the situational pressures that would make ordinary people comply. Naive realism made them confident in a prediction that was catastrophically wrong.

## Real-world examples

**Political disagreement.** Both sides in any political debate genuinely believe they're seeing reality clearly and the other side is deluded. Naive realism makes each side underestimate the legitimate reasons the other might disagree.

**Workplace conflict.** Two colleagues interpret the same meeting differently and each believes their interpretation is the "real" one. Neither considers that their interpretation is shaped by their position, history, and expectations.

## When to use it

Apply naive realism awareness in any disagreement. Ask: "What if I'm not seeing this objectively either? What information, experience, or perspective does the other person have that might make their position rational from their viewpoint?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is intellectual — understanding naive realism in theory but still being convinced that *you* see things clearly (it's always other people who are naive realists). The bias is, by definition, invisible to the person who has it.

## Try it now

Think of a strong disagreement you're currently in. Instead of building your case, try to construct the version of reality in which the other person's position makes complete sense. What would you have to believe for their view to be rational?
