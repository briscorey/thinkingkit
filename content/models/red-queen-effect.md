## How it works

Named after the Red Queen in Lewis Carroll's *Through the Looking-Glass* — "It takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place" — this model describes situations where competing entities must continuously improve just to maintain their relative position.

In biology, predators evolve faster legs, so prey evolves faster legs too. Neither gains lasting advantage; both just run faster. In business, as every company adopts the same "best practices," those practices stop being advantages and become table stakes.

The practical implication: most improvements just keep you at parity. True competitive advantage requires doing something different, not just doing the same thing better.

## Case study: How the cybersecurity arms race mirrors evolutionary biology

The Red Queen Effect — named after the character in Through the Looking-Glass who must run faster and faster just to stay in place — perfectly describes the relationship between cybersecurity defenders and attackers.

In the 1990s, basic firewalls and antivirus software were sufficient protection. Attackers evolved. Defenders built intrusion detection systems. Attackers developed polymorphic malware that changed its signature to evade detection. Defenders deployed machine learning-based threat detection. Attackers used adversarial machine learning to fool the detectors. Each advance by one side is immediately countered by the other.

Companies that stop investing in security don't remain at their current level — they fall behind, because the threat landscape continues evolving. The Red Queen Effect means that in competitive or adversarial systems, standing still is equivalent to falling behind. You must keep running just to maintain your current position.

## Real-world examples

**Technology.** Every company now has a website, social media, and email marketing. These were competitive advantages in 2005. Today they're minimum requirements. The companies that invested early gained a temporary edge that was erased when everyone caught up.

**Education.** As more people get university degrees, the value of a degree falls. Now a master's degree is needed for positions that once required only a bachelor's. The average education level rises but relative advantage stays flat.

**Skills.** Learning to code was a distinctive advantage 15 years ago. Now it's an expected skill in many non-technical roles. The Red Queen means you need to keep levelling up just to maintain your market position.

## When to use it

Apply this model when evaluating whether an improvement will create lasting advantage or merely temporary parity. When everyone can copy your innovation, the advantage is temporary. Ask: "Is this a Red Queen improvement (keeping up) or a genuine differentiator (getting ahead)?" Plan your effort allocation accordingly.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is concluding that improvement is pointless because competitors will copy you. Stopping improvement in a Red Queen race doesn't leave you in place — it leaves you behind. You must run to stay still, and run faster to get ahead. The second mistake is spending all your energy on Red Queen improvements when you should allocate some to genuinely asymmetric moves.
