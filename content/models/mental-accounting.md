## How it works

Mental accounting, identified by Richard Thaler, describes how people create separate mental "accounts" for their money and treat funds differently depending on which account they're in. A bonus feels like "free money" and gets spent more freely than salary. A tax refund feels like a windfall even though it's money you overpaid. Casino winnings feel like "house money" and get risked more aggressively.

Economically, this is irrational — money is fungible, meaning a dollar is a dollar regardless of its source. But psychologically, we consistently violate this principle.

## Real-world examples

**The bonus effect.** You carefully budget your monthly salary but blow through a $2,000 bonus on an impulse purchase you'd never make with "regular" money. Same $2,000, different mental treatment.

**Sunk cost version.** You've already spent $100 on a concert ticket. The night arrives and you feel terrible. You go anyway because you "already paid for it" — even though the $100 is gone regardless and you'd be happier staying home.

## When to use it

Catch mental accounting whenever you notice yourself treating money differently based on its source, label, or account. Ask: "Would I make this same decision if this money came from my regular income?" If not, you're being influenced by the label, not the value.

## Common mistakes

Mental accounting isn't always bad. Budgeting categories — rent, savings, entertainment — are a form of deliberate mental accounting that helps with financial discipline. The problem arises when invisible mental accounts lead to inconsistent decisions.

## Try it now

Think about the last time you received "unexpected" money — a refund, a gift, a bonus. How did you spend it? Would you have spent the same amount from your savings account on the same thing? If not, mental accounting was at work.
