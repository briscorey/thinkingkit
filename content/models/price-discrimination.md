## How it works

Price discrimination means charging different prices to different customers for the same product, based on their willingness to pay. Airlines do it (business class vs economy for the same flight), software companies do it (student pricing, enterprise pricing), and movie theatres do it (matinee discounts, senior pricing).

For price discrimination to work, the seller needs to segment customers by willingness to pay, prevent resale between segments (a student can't buy the cheap version and sell it to a business), and have some market power (competitive markets make discrimination harder).

## Case study: How pharmaceutical companies charge Americans 3x more than Canadians for identical drugs

The same medication — identical molecule, identical manufacturer, identical packaging — costs an average of 2-3x more in the United States than in Canada, Europe, or Australia. This isn't a cost difference — it's price discrimination based on willingness and ability to pay.

U.S. law prevents Medicare from negotiating drug prices and restricts importation from Canada, effectively segmenting the market. American consumers can't access the Canadian price, and Canadian consumers can't resell at the American price. The pharmaceutical companies capture maximum value from each segment — the definition of successful price discrimination.

## When to use it

Apply price discrimination awareness whenever the same product costs different amounts in different contexts. Ask: "Am I paying more because the product costs more to deliver to me, or because the seller knows I'll pay more?"

## Try it now

Check the price of a product you use regularly across different purchasing channels (student vs regular, annual vs monthly, US vs international). How much of the price difference reflects cost differences, and how much is pure segmentation?
