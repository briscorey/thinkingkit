## How it works

The gambler's fallacy is the belief that past random events affect the probability of future random events. If a fair coin lands heads five times in a row, many people feel that tails is "due" — that the probability of tails has somehow increased. It hasn't. The coin has no memory. Each flip is independent. The probability of tails remains exactly 50%.

The fallacy arises because we intuitively expect small samples to look like the overall distribution. Five heads in a row doesn't "feel" random, so we expect the next flip to correct the pattern. But randomness is under no obligation to look balanced in small samples.

## Case study: How the Monte Carlo Casino's roulette streak of 1913 cost gamblers millions

On August 18, 1913, the ball at a Monte Carlo Casino roulette table landed on black 26 consecutive times. As the streak continued, gamblers piled increasingly large bets on red, convinced that red was "overdue." Each spin, they doubled down.

The casino made millions. The probability of black on any individual spin remained exactly 18/37 (about 48.6%) regardless of what had come before. The streak was unusual but not impossible — and critically, it said nothing about the next spin. The gamblers were literally betting their money on the gambler's fallacy.

## When to use it

Apply gambler's fallacy awareness whenever you're tempted to see patterns in random sequences. Ask: "Does this system actually have memory? Do past outcomes actually influence future probabilities?" In truly random systems, the answer is no.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is the inverse: concluding that streaks never carry information. In some systems, past performance does predict future results (skilled athletes, momentum in markets, positive feedback loops). The gambler's fallacy applies specifically to independent random events, not to systems with memory or feedback.

## Try it now

Think of a streak you've experienced recently — good luck or bad luck. Ask: "Is this genuinely random (like coin flips), or is there a causal mechanism that might continue the streak?" If random, resist the urge to bet on reversal.
