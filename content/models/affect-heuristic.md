## How it works

The affect heuristic means making judgments based on your current emotional response rather than systematic analysis. If something feels scary, you overestimate its risk. If it feels pleasant, you underestimate the costs. Your feelings become a shortcut for analysis — fast but often inaccurate.

Paul Slovic's research showed that when people have a positive feeling toward an activity (like nuclear power among its supporters), they rate both its benefits as high and its risks as low. When they have a negative feeling, they rate benefits as low and risks as high. The emotional response drives both assessments, even though benefits and risks are logically independent.

## Case study: How fear of flying versus driving kills thousands more people per year

After the September 11, 2001 attacks, millions of Americans switched from flying to driving for their travels. Flying felt terrifying — the emotional response to the attacks was overwhelming. Driving felt safe and familiar.

Gerd Gigerenzer calculated that the shift from flying to driving caused approximately 1,600 additional road deaths in the year following 9/11 — more than six times the number who died in the attacks. The affect heuristic — flying feels scary, driving feels safe — led to a decision that was statistically far more dangerous. The feeling and the reality were inverted.

## When to use it

Apply affect heuristic awareness whenever you notice a strong emotional response to a risk or opportunity. Ask: "Am I assessing this based on data, or based on how it makes me feel?"

## Common mistakes

The mistake is concluding that emotions are always wrong. Sometimes your emotional response captures genuine information that your analysis misses. The skill is knowing when feelings are signal and when they're noise.

## Try it now

Think of something you consider risky and something you consider safe. Now look up the actual statistics. Does the data match your emotional assessment, or has the affect heuristic been steering you wrong?
