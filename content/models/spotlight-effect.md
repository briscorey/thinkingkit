## How it works

Thomas Gilovich and colleagues demonstrated that we consistently overestimate how much other people notice and remember about our appearance, behaviour, and mistakes. In one study, students were asked to wear an embarrassing T-shirt (featuring Barry Manilow) to a room full of peers. The wearers estimated that 50% of people in the room noticed the shirt. The actual number: 25%.

The spotlight effect occurs because we're the centre of our own world. We notice everything about ourselves, so we assume others do too. They don't — they're busy being the centre of their own world.

## Case study: How the spotlight effect costs presenters hours of unnecessary anxiety

Public speaking coaches report that the vast majority of speaker anxiety comes from imagined audience judgment about specific details: a stumbled word, a wrinkled shirt, a nervous gesture. Post-presentation surveys consistently show that audiences rarely notice these details and remember almost none of them.

Research at Cornell University found that audience members recalled the overall message and emotional tone of presentations, but almost never specific "mistakes" that the presenter had agonised over. The spotlight that felt blinding to the speaker was essentially invisible to the audience. The hours spent worrying about minor imperfections were hours spent preparing for a judgment that would never come.

## When to use it

Apply spotlight effect awareness whenever you feel self-conscious or are avoiding action because of how you think others will perceive you. Ask: "Am I imagining more scrutiny than actually exists? Would I even notice this in someone else?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is using the spotlight effect to justify genuinely poor behaviour ("nobody notices anyway"). People do notice egregious things. The spotlight effect applies to the minor imperfections we obsess over, not to fundamental quality.

## Try it now

Think of the last time you were embarrassed in public. How many people do you think noticed? Now ask: can you remember anyone else's embarrassing moment from that same week? Probably not — and they can't remember yours either.
