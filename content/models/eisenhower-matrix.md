## How it works

Dwight Eisenhower reportedly said: "What is important is seldom urgent and what is urgent is seldom important." Stephen Covey popularised this insight as a 2×2 matrix.

**Quadrant 1 — Urgent and Important.** Crises, deadlines, emergencies. You must do these. But if you live here, you're constantly firefighting.

**Quadrant 2 — Important but Not Urgent.** Strategy, relationships, learning, prevention, planning. This is where the highest-leverage work lives. It never screams for attention, so it gets neglected.

**Quadrant 3 — Urgent but Not Important.** Most emails, many meetings, other people's priorities disguised as yours. These feel productive but aren't.

**Quadrant 4 — Neither Urgent nor Important.** Time-wasting activities. Pleasant escapes. Eliminate or minimise.

The insight: most people spend too much time in Quadrants 1 and 3, and not enough in Quadrant 2. Systematically shifting time toward Q2 work — before it becomes Q1 — is the highest-leverage productivity move available.

## Real-world examples

**Teaching.** Urgent: marking the pile of essays due tomorrow (Q1). Important but not urgent: redesigning the unit plan to be more effective next year (Q2). Urgent but not important: responding to the non-essential email chain (Q3). Neither: scrolling through a resource website without a specific goal (Q4).

**Health.** Q1: Going to the doctor for acute pain. Q2: Regular exercise, sleep hygiene, preventive check-ups. Q3: Researching the latest supplement trend everyone's talking about. Q4: Reading arguments about diet fads online.

## When to use it

Use the Eisenhower Matrix at the start of each week to audit where your time actually goes. It's most powerful when you feel busy but unproductive, when your to-do list grows faster than you complete it, and when you know you should be working on long-term projects but "never have time."

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is categorising tasks by how they feel rather than what they are. Emails feel urgent. They rarely are. The second mistake is treating Q2 work as a luxury rather than a necessity. If you only do Q2 work "when you have time," you never will.

## Try it now

List your top 10 tasks for this week. Place each one in a quadrant. How many are in Q2? If fewer than three, you're probably spending too much time on other people's priorities. What's one Q2 task you could start this week?
