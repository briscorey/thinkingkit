## How it works

Humans compulsively construct stories to explain random events, creating false causation and illusory patterns.

This model helps you recognise situations where this pattern is at play and adjust your thinking accordingly. Understanding it does not make you immune, but it gives you a framework for catching it in action and making better decisions as a result.

## Case study: How the media constructed a false narrative around Enron

Before its collapse in 2001, Enron was celebrated as the most innovative company in America. Fortune named it "America's Most Innovative Company" for six consecutive years. Business journalists constructed a compelling narrative: visionary leadership, revolutionary business model, the future of energy markets.

After the fraud was revealed, the same journalists reconstructed the story with equal confidence. Now the narrative was obvious corruption, red flags everywhere, a house of cards that anyone should have seen through.

Both narratives were constructed after the fact. Before the collapse, the data was selectively arranged to tell a success story. After the collapse, the same data was rearranged to tell a failure story. The underlying reality hadn't changed — only the narrative imposed upon it. The human need for coherent stories made both versions feel obvious and inevitable in retrospect.

## When to use it

Apply this model whenever you notice its pattern appearing in your decision-making, your team's behaviour, or the systems around you. It is especially valuable in high-stakes situations where the cost of this error is significant.
