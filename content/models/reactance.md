## How it works

When people feel their freedom or autonomy is being threatened — told they can't do something, or must do something — they often react by doing the opposite, even if compliance would benefit them. This isn't stubbornness for its own sake: it's a deep psychological need to protect perceived freedom of choice.

The stronger the perceived threat to freedom, the stronger the reactance. Banning something makes it more desirable. Mandating something makes it less appealing. This is why "reverse psychology" works — by appearing to restrict a choice, you can actually increase desire for it.

## Case study: How Prohibition increased alcohol consumption and created organised crime

The 18th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution, ratified in 1919, banned the production, sale, and transportation of alcohol. The intention was to reduce drinking, improve public health, and decrease crime.

The result was the opposite on every dimension. Alcohol consumption, after an initial dip, returned to near pre-Prohibition levels but moved underground. The ban created a $3 billion illegal market (roughly $50 billion today), funded the rise of organised crime empires under figures like Al Capone, and corrupted law enforcement agencies across the country. When Prohibition was repealed in 1933, drinking actually decreased — because removing the prohibition removed the reactance.

## When to use it

Apply reactance awareness when trying to persuade or influence. Instead of direct mandates ("you must do this"), offer choices ("which of these approaches would you prefer?"). Preserve the other person's sense of autonomy.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is assuming reactance is always irrational. Sometimes pushback against authority is legitimate and necessary. Reactance is problematic when it causes people to reject genuinely good options purely because they were told to accept them.

## Try it now

Think of a time you resisted something primarily because you felt pressured into it. Was your resistance based on the merits, or on the feeling of your freedom being threatened? How would you have responded if the same option had been presented as a suggestion rather than a mandate?
