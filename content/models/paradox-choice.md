## How it works

Barry Schwartz's research shows that increasing the number of options doesn't always increase satisfaction. Beyond a threshold, more options lead to decision paralysis (unable to choose), regret (constantly wondering if you chose wrong), and elevated expectations (with so many options, surely the perfect one exists).

The distinction is between "maximisers" (who want the absolute best option) and "satisficers" (who want the first option that meets their criteria). Satisficers make faster decisions and report higher satisfaction, despite spending less effort choosing.

## Real-world examples

**Streaming services.** With thousands of shows available, many people spend 30 minutes browsing and end up watching nothing — or watching something and feeling they should have chosen better.

**Career choices.** Previous generations had fewer career options and less career anxiety. More options creates more opportunity but also more doubt and FOMO.

## When to use it

Apply the paradox of choice when you notice decision fatigue or chronic dissatisfaction with choices. Ask: "Would fewer options make this decision easier and the outcome more satisfying?" Consider deliberately limiting your options before choosing.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is concluding that choice is bad. It's not — having options is vastly better than having none. The paradox is specifically about excessive choice beyond the useful threshold. The fix is not fewer options in the world, but a personal strategy for handling abundance (like satisficing).

## Try it now

Identify a decision where you've been stuck in analysis mode. Define three "good enough" criteria. Does an option exist that meets all three? If yes, choose it now and stop searching.
