## How it works

In any stream of information, a small fraction is signal — data that actually changes your understanding or should change your behaviour — and the rest is noise — random variation that looks meaningful but isn't.

Nassim Taleb demonstrates this mathematically: if you check a volatile investment daily, you'll see roughly 50% positive and 50% negative days, generating enormous emotional noise. Check it annually, and you'll see a much clearer signal of the long-term trend. The underlying reality hasn't changed — only how much noise you're exposed to.

The principle applies to news, social media, metrics, feedback, market data, and any information stream. More data doesn't always mean more signal. Often it means proportionally more noise.

## Real-world examples

**News.** Daily news is almost entirely noise. Very little of what's reported today will matter in a year. Monthly or quarterly summaries contain far more signal per word.

**Business metrics.** Checking website traffic hourly produces noise. Weekly trends show signal. Reacting to daily fluctuations leads to constant, counterproductive interventions.

## When to use it

Apply signal-noise thinking whenever you're consuming information or monitoring metrics. Ask: "Would checking this less frequently improve my decision-making by reducing noise exposure?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is filtering so aggressively that you miss genuine signals. The goal isn't to ignore all new information — it's to calibrate your exposure to the right frequency for the domain.

## Try it now

Identify one information source you check frequently (email, news, metrics, social media). What would happen if you checked it half as often? Would you actually miss anything important, or would you just experience less noise?
