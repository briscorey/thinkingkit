## How it works

Hindsight bias is the tendency to see past events as having been predictable, even when there was no way to know what would happen. Once you know the outcome, your brain rewrites the story to make it feel inevitable.

This matters because it makes us overconfident in our ability to predict the future, undervalues the role of luck and uncertainty, makes it harder to learn from mistakes (since we believe we "should have known"), and leads to unfair blame.

## Case study: How 9/11 made intelligence failures seem 'obvious' in retrospect

After the September 11, 2001 attacks, the 9/11 Commission identified numerous intelligence signals that, in retrospect, pointed toward the attacks: suspicious flight school enrollments, FBI field reports about potential hijackers, a presidential daily brief titled "Bin Ladin Determined to Strike in US."

In hindsight, these signals seemed blindingly obvious. The narrative of intelligence failure — "they should have known" — became the dominant public understanding. But intelligence analysts at the time were processing thousands of threat signals daily. The relevant signals were buried in noise, and their significance only became clear after the event revealed which signals mattered.

Roberta Wohlstetter's analysis of the Pearl Harbor attack had identified the same pattern decades earlier: in hindsight, the signals are obvious because you know which ones to look for. In real time, they're lost in a sea of irrelevant data. Hindsight bias makes every catastrophe seem preventable — which unfairly blames the people who couldn't know what we now know.

## Real-world examples

**Investing.** After a stock crashes, everyone says the warning signs were obvious. But at the time, most experts were bullish. The "obvious" warning signs only seem obvious in retrospect.

**Medical diagnosis.** After a diagnosis is confirmed, doctors reviewing the case often believe the symptoms clearly pointed to the correct answer, even when the initial presentation was genuinely ambiguous.

## When to use it

Use awareness of hindsight bias whenever you're reviewing past decisions (yours or others'), when assigning blame for failures, when evaluating predictions, and when writing post-mortems.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is overcorrecting — concluding that nothing is predictable. Some outcomes were foreseeable. The point is to be honest about which ones, and to judge past decisions by the information that was available at the time, not the outcome.

## Try it now

Think of a recent surprise — a project failure, an election result, a market move. Ask yourself: before it happened, what probability would you honestly have assigned to that outcome? Would you bet money at those odds?
