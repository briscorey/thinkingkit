## How it works

Humans systematically prefer smaller, sooner rewards over larger, later ones — and the preference isn't proportional to the delay. The discount rate is hyperbolic, not exponential: we're much more impatient about immediate versus slightly delayed rewards than about delayed versus even more delayed rewards.

Most people prefer $100 today over $110 tomorrow. But the same people would prefer $110 in 31 days over $100 in 30 days — even though the choice is structurally identical (wait one day for $10 more). The immediacy of "today" creates disproportionate urgency.

## Case study: How marshmallow test children's self-control predicted life outcomes

Walter Mischel's famous 1972 Stanford marshmallow experiment offered preschoolers a choice: one marshmallow now, or two marshmallows if they waited 15 minutes. About a third managed to wait.

Follow-up studies decades later found that the children who waited scored an average of 210 points higher on their SATs, had lower rates of substance abuse, lower divorce rates, and lower BMIs. The ability to resist hyperbolic discounting — to choose the larger-later reward — correlated with better outcomes across virtually every life domain.

The mechanism isn't mysterious: compounding rewards (in education, savings, relationships, health) require sustained investment in larger-later payoffs. People who systematically choose smaller-sooner payoffs forgo the compound returns.

## When to use it

Apply hyperbolic discounting awareness whenever you're tempted by an immediate reward. Ask: "Would I make this same choice if both options were equally delayed? If $100 in 30 days vs $110 in 31 days is obvious, why is $100 today vs $110 tomorrow different?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is assuming all impatience is irrational. Sometimes the immediate option genuinely has a higher expected value (bird in the hand). Hyperbolic discounting is specifically the disproportionate preference for immediacy that disappears when both options are displaced into the future.

## Try it now

Identify one area where you consistently choose the smaller-sooner reward (snacking vs cooking, scrolling vs reading, spending vs saving). What would the compounded value of the larger-later choice be over a year?
