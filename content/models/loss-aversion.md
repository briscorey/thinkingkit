## How it works

Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory demonstrated that humans don't evaluate outcomes symmetrically. A $100 loss feels roughly twice as painful as a $100 gain feels good. This means we're not rational expected-value calculators — we're loss-avoiding machines.

This asymmetry creates predictable decision errors. We hold losing investments too long (selling would crystallise the loss). We avoid beneficial risks (the potential loss looms larger than the potential gain). We overpay for insurance and warranties. We stick with the status quo even when change would be beneficial, because change involves potential losses.

## Real-world examples

**Investing.** The "disposition effect" — investors sell winners too early (to lock in gains) and hold losers too long (to avoid realising losses). This is loss aversion in action, and it systematically reduces portfolio returns.

**Negotiations.** Framing matters enormously. "You'll save $200" is less motivating than "You'll lose $200 if you don't act." Same economics, different psychological impact. Loss framing is consistently more powerful.

**Career.** People stay in jobs they dislike because leaving means losing status, seniority, and social connections — even when the new opportunity offers greater gains. The losses feel more concrete than the gains.

## When to use it

Check for loss aversion whenever you're avoiding a decision that, on paper, has positive expected value. Whenever you're clinging to something (a position, an investment, a relationship) primarily because leaving it would feel like losing. And whenever you're presented with a decision framed in terms of potential losses — reframe it in terms of gains to see if your preference changes.

## Common mistakes

Loss aversion isn't always irrational. If a loss would be catastrophic (losing your house, your health), then weighting it heavily relative to an equivalent gain is perfectly reasonable. The mistake is applying catastrophic-loss psychology to everyday, recoverable decisions.

## Try it now

Think of a decision you've been avoiding. Write down what you'd gain if it works out and what you'd lose if it doesn't. Now honestly assess: is your reluctance proportional to the actual magnitude of the potential loss, or is loss aversion magnifying it? Would you advise a friend differently than you're advising yourself?
