## How it works

G.K. Chesterton proposed a simple rule: before you remove a fence (or any existing rule, institution, or practice), you must first understand why it was put there. If you can't explain the reason for its existence, you're not yet qualified to remove it.

The principle applies to any inherited structure — company policies, social norms, legal regulations, architectural features, cultural traditions. The fact that something seems useless or outdated doesn't mean it is. It may serve a purpose you haven't identified yet.

This is a conservative heuristic in the best sense: it doesn't say "never change anything." It says "understand before you change."

## Real-world examples

**Regulations.** A new executive looks at a complex compliance process and says "this is bureaucratic waste — let's cut it." Later, the company faces a lawsuit because the process existed to protect against exactly that legal exposure.

**Code.** A developer sees a strange function in the codebase that seems to do nothing useful and deletes it. Production breaks because the function handled an edge case that occurs once a month.

## When to use it

Apply Chesterton's Fence before removing, changing, or disregarding any inherited system, rule, or practice. Ask: "Why does this exist? What problem was it originally solving? Is that problem still present?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is using this as an excuse to never change anything. Chesterton's Fence doesn't say fences should never be removed — it says you should understand them first. Once you understand why the fence exists, you may well decide to remove it.

## Try it now

Identify a rule, policy, or practice at your workplace that seems pointless. Before advocating to change it, find out why it was created. Talk to someone who was there when it was established. You might discover it's genuinely outdated — or you might discover a problem you didn't know existed.
