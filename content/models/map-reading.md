## How it works

Before acting in any situation, assess the actual dynamics at play: who are the stakeholders? What are their incentives? Where does the power sit? What are the unwritten rules? The same action produces completely different results in different contexts.

Reading the room isn't manipulation — it's due diligence. A surgeon who doesn't assess the patient before operating is reckless, not brave. A leader who doesn't assess the organisational dynamics before intervening is equally reckless.

## Case study: How Satya Nadella read Microsoft's culture before attempting to change it

When Satya Nadella became Microsoft CEO in 2014, the company was known for a toxic internal culture of infighting, stack ranking, and "not invented here" syndrome. Previous attempts to change the culture through mandates had failed.

Nadella spent his first months listening — attending meetings across the company, having one-on-one conversations with employees at every level, understanding the actual incentive structures and power dynamics. Only after mapping the real landscape did he begin making changes, starting with removing stack ranking (the structural cause of infighting) rather than lecturing about collaboration.

Microsoft's subsequent transformation — from stagnant also-ran to the world's most valuable company — happened because Nadella read the room before trying to change it.

## When to use it

Apply room-reading before any intervention in a social or organisational system. Ask: "Do I understand the actual dynamics here — incentives, relationships, power structures, unwritten rules — well enough to predict how my intervention will be received?"

## Try it now

Think of a situation where you want to make a change. Before acting, map the stakeholders, their incentives, and the likely second-order effects of your action. Does the map suggest your planned approach will work, or does it reveal dynamics you haven't accounted for?
