## How it works

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky identified the planning fallacy: people systematically underestimate the time, cost, and risk of future actions while overestimating their benefits. This happens even when they have direct experience of past projects taking longer than expected.

The mechanism: when planning, we focus on the specific scenario we envision (the "inside view") rather than the base rate of similar projects (the "outside view"). We imagine everything going right and build our estimate around that ideal scenario.

The antidote: use the outside view. Instead of estimating from your plan, look at how long similar projects actually took in the past. That historical base rate is almost always a better predictor than your current optimistic plan.

## Real-world examples

**Software development.** Developers estimate a feature will take two weeks. It takes six. This isn't an anomaly — it's the pattern. Studies show software projects average 2-3x their initial estimates.

**Home renovation.** The contractor says eight weeks. Every homeowner who has ever renovated knows the true number is higher. The planning fallacy is so reliable in construction that experienced project managers simply multiply estimates by 1.5-2x.

## When to use it

Apply the planning fallacy correction to every significant time or cost estimate. Ask: "How long did similar things actually take in the past?" Use that historical data, not your optimistic inside-view estimate.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is knowing about the planning fallacy and still failing to correct for it. The bias is remarkably resistant to awareness. The most effective countermeasure is mechanical: take your estimate and multiply it by a correction factor based on historical data.

## Try it now

Think of your current most important project. What's your estimated completion date? Now recall the last three similar projects. How long did they actually take versus their estimates? Apply that ratio to your current estimate. What's the adjusted date?
