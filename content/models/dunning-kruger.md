## How it works

The Dunning-Kruger effect describes an asymmetry in self-assessment. When you know very little about a domain, you lack the knowledge needed to recognise how little you know. You don't know what you don't know. As you gain competence, you begin to see the full scope of the domain and your confidence drops — you now understand how much more there is to learn. With expertise comes a more accurate (and often more modest) self-assessment.

This isn't about intelligence. It's about the relationship between competence and metacognition in any specific domain. A brilliant physicist can be Dunning-Kruger'd about nutrition. An expert chef can be Dunning-Kruger'd about investing.

## Real-world examples

**New managers.** A first-time manager often feels confident about their leadership abilities — they've watched other managers for years, how hard can it be? After six months of actually managing, their confidence often drops sharply as they discover the hidden complexity of the role.

**Learning a language.** After a week of lessons, tourists feel surprisingly confident ordering food. After six months of study, they realise how vast the language actually is and feel less confident despite knowing far more.

## When to use it

Apply this model as a self-check whenever you feel unusually confident about something you haven't spent much time on. Also apply it in reverse: if you feel uncertain despite significant experience, that uncertainty may actually be a sign of genuine expertise rather than incompetence.

## Common mistakes

The biggest misuse of this model is as a weapon: dismissing someone's opinion by saying "that's just Dunning-Kruger." The effect describes a statistical tendency across populations, not a diagnostic tool for individuals. The second mistake is assuming experts are always right. Expert underconfidence is real, but so is expert overconfidence when they step outside their domain.

## Try it now

Pick two domains: one where you have deep experience and one where you have casual familiarity. Rate your competence in each from 1-10. Now ask someone knowledgeable in each field to rate you. The gap between your self-assessment and their assessment reveals your blind spots.
