## How it works

Hysteresis means that a system's current state depends on its history, not just its current inputs. Even if you remove the cause of a change, the system doesn't necessarily return to its original state. The path matters, and some changes leave permanent marks.

A rubber band stretched too far doesn't return to its original shape. A reputation damaged by scandal doesn't fully recover when the truth emerges. A relationship strained by betrayal doesn't return to baseline when an apology is given.

## Case study: How the 2008 recession permanently changed millennial economic behaviour

The millennials who entered the workforce during the 2008 recession earned 10-15% less than those who graduated just a few years earlier. Standard economic theory predicted they would catch up as the economy recovered. Many haven't — twenty years later, the cohort still shows lower earnings, lower homeownership, lower marriage rates, and higher risk aversion.

The recession was a temporary input. The behavioural changes it created were permanent — hysteresis. The experience of economic precarity during formative years permanently altered spending habits, career choices, and attitudes toward risk. Removing the recession didn't remove its effects.

## When to use it

Apply hysteresis thinking when assessing whether damage is truly reversible. Ask: "Even if I fix the cause, will the system return to its previous state, or has the change become permanent?"

## Try it now

Think of a setback in your career, relationships, or health. Has the system fully recovered since the cause was addressed, or are there lasting effects? If lasting, that's hysteresis — and the appropriate response is adaptation rather than restoration.
