## How it works

When someone does something that hurts you, your brain's first instinct is to assume intent. They *meant* to undermine you. They *wanted* to make you look bad. They're *out to get you*.

Hanlon's Razor challenges this instinct. Most of the time, the person wasn't being malicious — they were being careless, busy, uninformed, or simply not thinking about the impact on you. People are far more self-absorbed than they are scheming.

This isn't naive optimism. It's a statistical bet. The base rate for genuine malice is much lower than the base rate for negligence, distraction, and miscommunication. When you assume malice incorrectly, you escalate conflicts unnecessarily. When you assume negligence, you open the door to resolution.

## Real-world examples

**Workplace.** Your colleague didn't CC you on an important email thread. Malice interpretation: they're trying to cut you out of the project. Hanlon's interpretation: they forgot, or the thread grew organically and they didn't think to add you. The Hanlon's approach lets you simply ask to be added, rather than starting a political counter-campaign.

**Relationships.** Your friend hasn't replied to your message in three days. Malice interpretation: they're angry at you and giving you the silent treatment. Hanlon's interpretation: they're busy, distracted, or the message got buried. One interpretation breeds resentment. The other breeds patience.

## When to use it

Apply Hanlon's Razor whenever you feel the urge to attribute someone's behaviour to hostile intent, when political interpretations of workplace behaviour start multiplying, and when you're about to escalate a conflict based on assumed motivations.

## Common mistakes

The biggest mistake is applying it universally. Hanlon's Razor is a default, not an absolute. Some people really are acting with malice. Repeated patterns of harmful behaviour, especially when someone has been informed of the impact, shift the probability toward intent. The razor says *start* by assuming incompetence — not that you should never update that assumption.

## Try it now

Think of a recent situation where someone's behaviour annoyed or hurt you. Write down your assumption about their motivation. Now rewrite the scenario assuming the most charitable explanation — they were distracted, uninformed, or didn't realise the impact. Which explanation fits the evidence better?
