## How it works

No single mental model works for every situation. The skill isn't knowing the most models — it's knowing which model fits which situation. A hammer is useless for screwing; a screwdriver is useless for hammering. Both are excellent tools. The craft is in the selection.

This meta-model asks: before applying a framework, check whether it's the right one for the problem at hand. Match the tool to the job, not the job to your favourite tool.

## Case study: How Abraham Maslow's hammer bias distorted decades of psychology

Abraham Maslow wrote in 1966: "If the only tool you have is a hammer, it is tempting to treat everything as if it were a nail." The irony is that Maslow himself demonstrated this bias — his hierarchy of needs, while useful for understanding motivation, was applied far beyond its valid domain to education, business management, marketing, and international development, often with poor results.

Management consultants applied Maslow's hierarchy to employee motivation as if it were a universal law rather than a rough heuristic developed from studying a specific population. When it didn't work in collectivist cultures (where belonging might precede esteem), practitioners blamed the culture rather than questioning the model's fit.

## When to use it

Apply this meta-model before reaching for any framework. Ask: "Is this the right tool for this specific situation? What assumptions does this model make, and do they hold here?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is model loyalty — using the same favourite framework for every problem because you know it well, not because it fits. The second mistake is model paralysis — having so many frameworks available that you can't choose one and act.

## Try it now

Think of a framework you use frequently. What are its assumptions? In what situations would those assumptions fail? What alternative model would work better in those situations?
