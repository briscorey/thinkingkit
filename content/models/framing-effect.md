## How it works

The same information presented differently leads to different decisions — even when the underlying facts are identical.

This model helps you recognise situations where this pattern is at play and adjust your thinking accordingly. Understanding it does not make you immune, but it gives you a framework for catching it in action and making better decisions as a result.

## Case study: How Amos Tversky changed medical decisions with one sentence

In a landmark 1981 study, Tversky and Kahneman presented doctors with identical treatment statistics framed two different ways. Treatment A: "The one-year survival rate is 90%." Treatment B: "There is 10% mortality in the first year." Same information. Same numbers.

When framed as survival, 82% of doctors chose the treatment. When framed as mortality, only 54% chose it. These were trained medical professionals making life-and-death decisions — and the way the information was presented changed their recommendation for nearly a third of them.

The finding has been replicated hundreds of times across domains. Whether presenting investment returns, policy outcomes, or medical risks, the frame changes the decision — even when the underlying facts are identical. The implication is unsettling: the person who chooses the frame often determines the decision.

## When to use it

Apply this model whenever you notice its pattern appearing in your decision-making, your team's behaviour, or the systems around you. It is especially valuable in high-stakes situations where the cost of this error is significant.
