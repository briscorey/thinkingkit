## How it works

The Principal-Agent Problem occurs whenever one person (the agent) acts on behalf of another (the principal), but their interests aren't perfectly aligned. The principal wants the agent to act in the principal's best interest, but the agent has their own goals — and the principal can't perfectly monitor what the agent does.

This isn't about bad people. It's about misaligned structures. A real estate agent technically works for you, but earns commission based on the sale price. A CEO technically works for shareholders, but is motivated by their own compensation, reputation, and job security. A doctor technically works for the patient, but practices within a system that rewards certain treatments over others.

## Real-world examples

**Financial advisors.** An advisor paid by commission earns more when you trade more often. An advisor paid a flat annual fee earns the same regardless. Same expertise, structurally different advice.

**Corporate management.** A CEO might resist a merger that would benefit shareholders because the merger would eliminate their job. Their interests as an agent diverge from their duty to the principals.

## When to use it

Apply principal-agent thinking whenever someone is acting on your behalf. Ask: "How is this person compensated? What happens to them if they give me bad advice? Are their incentives aligned with my interests or opposed to them?"

## Common mistakes

The mistake is assuming agents are deliberately dishonest. Usually they're not — they genuinely believe their recommendations are good. The bias operates through motivated reasoning, not deception.

## Try it now

Think of a professional relationship where someone advises or acts on your behalf. What's their compensation structure? If their incentives were different, would their advice change?
