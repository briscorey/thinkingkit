## How it works

Process-outcome thinking separates the quality of a decision from the quality of its result. Good decisions can produce bad outcomes (a well-researched investment that loses money due to unforeseeable events). Bad decisions can produce good outcomes (a reckless gamble that happens to pay off).

Over time, good processes produce better outcomes than bad processes. But any single outcome is a noisy signal about process quality. Judging decisions solely by outcomes leads to learning the wrong lessons — reinforcing lucky bad processes and abandoning unlucky good ones.

## Case study: How the 2003 World Series of Poker rewarded the worst decision in poker history

In the 2003 World Series of Poker, amateur Chris Moneymaker called an all-in bet from professional Sam Farha with a weak hand. Mathematically, it was a terrible call — Moneymaker was a significant underdog. He won anyway, went on to win the tournament, and sparked the poker boom.

Millions of amateur players saw Moneymaker's outcome and concluded that his aggressive, loose style was the correct strategy. They confused the outcome (he won) with the process (his call was mathematically wrong). The pros who lost to Moneymaker had better processes — they just experienced an unlikely outcome. Over thousands of hands, the pros' better processes would dominate. But the TV audience learned from one hand's result.

## When to use it

Apply process-outcome thinking after every important decision. Ask: "Given what I knew at the time, was the reasoning sound?" Judge past decisions by the quality of the thinking, not the quality of the luck.

## Try it now

Think of a recent decision that produced a bad outcome. Was the process actually bad, or was the outcome unlucky? If you'd make the same decision again with the same information, the process was good — the outcome was just noise.
