## How it works

The Devil's Advocate technique means deliberately arguing the opposing position — not because you believe it, but to stress-test the current plan. The name comes from the Catholic Church, which appointed an advocate to argue against a candidate's sainthood to ensure only truly deserving cases succeeded.

In practice, assign someone (or yourself) the explicit role of finding flaws. This works because social dynamics normally suppress dissent — people don't want to be the one who disagrees. The Devil's Advocate role gives them permission and obligation to push back.

## Real-world examples

**Strategy meetings.** Before committing to a product direction, one team member is assigned to make the strongest possible case against it. If the plan survives the attack, confidence is justified. If it doesn't, you've saved months of wasted effort.

**Hiring decisions.** After an enthusiastic interview, ask someone to argue why this candidate would be a bad hire. This counterbalances the halo effect and confirmation bias that typically dominate hiring discussions.

## When to use it

Use the Devil's Advocate before any major commitment — product launches, strategic pivots, hiring decisions, large investments. It's especially valuable when the group seems unanimous, since unanimity often signals groupthink rather than genuine agreement.

## Common mistakes

The biggest mistake is making the Devil's Advocate role symbolic rather than genuine. If the person arguing against doesn't truly try to find the best counterarguments, the exercise becomes theatre. The second mistake is always assigning the same person — rotate the role so it doesn't become associated with one "negative" personality.

## Try it now

Take a decision you've already made or are leaning toward. Spend three minutes writing the strongest possible argument against it. What's the best case someone could make that you're wrong? Does it change your confidence level?
