## How it works

During World War II, the military examined returning bombers to see where they'd been hit and considered reinforcing those areas. Mathematician Abraham Wald pointed out the flaw: the planes that were hit in other areas never returned. The bullet holes they were studying showed where planes could take damage and survive, not where they were most vulnerable.

This is survivorship bias: concentrating on things that survived a selection process while ignoring those that didn't, which leads to false conclusions about what causes survival.

We see success stories everywhere because failures are invisible. The successful entrepreneur is on the cover of magazines; the thousands who tried the same approach and failed are not. This creates a systematically distorted picture of what works.

## Case study: How Abraham Wald saved Allied bombers by looking at the missing data

During World War II, the U.S. military examined bombers returning from missions to determine where to add armour plating. The data showed heavy damage patterns on the fuselage and wings. The obvious conclusion: reinforce those areas.

Mathematician Abraham Wald proposed the opposite. The planes they were examining were the ones that survived. The damage on these planes showed where a bomber could take hits and still fly home. The missing data — the planes that didn't return — would show damage in different places: the engines and cockpit.

Wald argued that the military should armour the areas with no damage on returning planes, because hits there were what brought planes down. The military followed his recommendation. Wald didn't have better data. He had a better understanding of the data he had — specifically, what was missing from it.

## Real-world examples

**Entrepreneurship.** "College dropouts who became billionaires" makes great headlines. But for every Gates or Zuckerberg, thousands of dropouts failed — they just don't get featured. The survival rate of the strategy is invisible.

**Architecture.** Old buildings seem well-built because the poorly-built ones already collapsed. We're looking at the survivors and concluding that "they don't build them like they used to" — when actually they built plenty of bad ones too.

**Self-help.** A successful person shares their morning routine, and millions copy it. But many unsuccessful people had the same routine. The routine may not be what caused the success — it just happened to be present in a survivor.

## When to use it

Apply survivorship bias checking whenever you're drawing conclusions from success stories alone, whenever you're copying what successful people do without knowing the failure rate of that approach, and whenever a dataset only includes things that passed a selection filter.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is over-correcting: concluding that because survivorship bias exists, no lessons can be drawn from success. You can learn from winners — you just need to also study the losers. The comparison between survivors and non-survivors is where the real lessons hide.

## Try it now

Think of advice you've taken from a success story. Now ask: "How many people followed this same advice and didn't succeed? What distinguished the survivors from the non-survivors?" If you can't answer that question, you're learning from survivorship bias.
