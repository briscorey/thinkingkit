## How it works

Adverse selection occurs when one party in a transaction has information the other doesn't, and this asymmetry causes the wrong people to self-select into the transaction. The classic example: people who know they're high-risk are more likely to buy insurance, driving up costs for everyone.

Unlike moral hazard (which changes behaviour after the transaction), adverse selection distorts who enters the transaction in the first place. The information asymmetry means the population that participates is systematically different from the general population — and usually in ways that disadvantage the less-informed party.

## Case study: How adverse selection nearly destroyed the individual health insurance market

Before the Affordable Care Act, individual health insurance in the U.S. suffered severe adverse selection. Healthy people, who estimated they wouldn't need much care, chose not to buy insurance. Sick people, who knew they'd need care, bought coverage enthusiastically.

This meant the insurance pool was disproportionately expensive to insure. Premiums rose to cover costs. As premiums rose, more healthy people dropped out, worsening the pool further. This "death spiral" caused individual insurance markets to collapse in several states. The ACA's individual mandate was designed specifically to combat adverse selection by forcing healthy people into the pool.

## When to use it

Watch for adverse selection whenever participation in something is voluntary and participants have private information about their own risk or quality. Ask: "Are the people choosing to participate systematically different from those who aren't?"

## Common mistakes

The mistake is confusing adverse selection with fraud. Adverse selection isn't dishonest — high-risk people buying insurance are acting rationally. The problem is structural, not moral.

## Try it now

Think of a voluntary system you participate in — a marketplace, a community, a program. Who self-selects in? Who self-selects out? Is the resulting population systematically different from what the system was designed for?
