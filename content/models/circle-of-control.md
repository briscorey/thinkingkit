## How it works

Focus your energy on what you can control, accept what you cannot, and learn to tell the difference.

This model helps you recognise situations where this pattern is at play and adjust your thinking accordingly. Understanding it does not make you immune, but it gives you a framework for catching it in action and making better decisions as a result.

## Case study: How James Stockdale survived seven years as a POW using Stoic philosophy

Vice Admiral James Stockdale was the highest-ranking U.S. military officer in the Hanoi Hilton prisoner-of-war camp during the Vietnam War. He was imprisoned for seven and a half years, tortured repeatedly, and denied any rights.

Stockdale survived by applying Epictetus's Stoic distinction between what he could and couldn't control. He couldn't control when he'd be released, whether he'd be tortured, or what his captors would do. He could control his response, his integrity, and how he led fellow prisoners. He focused entirely on the latter.

When asked who didn't survive the camp, Stockdale answered: "The optimists. They were the ones who said, 'We'll be out by Christmas.' Christmas would come and go. Then Easter. Then Thanksgiving. And they died of a broken heart." The prisoners who survived were neither optimists nor pessimists — they were realists who confronted the brutal facts while maintaining faith that they would prevail in the end. They focused their energy on what they could control.

## When to use it

Apply this model whenever you notice its pattern appearing in your decision-making, your team's behaviour, or the systems around you. It is especially valuable in high-stakes situations where the cost of this error is significant.
