## How it works

Rene Girard's theory of mimetic desire proposes that we don't generate our desires independently — we borrow them from other people. We want things because we see others wanting them. The model (the person we're imitating) isn't just influencing our behaviour; they're generating our desires.

This explains why desire is often competitive and conflictual. When two people want the same thing because they're imitating each other's desire, they become rivals — even if the object itself isn't scarce. The rivalry, not the object, becomes the focus.

## Real-world examples

**Career ambitions.** You didn't want to become a consultant until your high-achieving friend became one. You didn't care about that promotion until you learned a colleague wanted it. Mimetic desire explains why people in similar social circles often want the same things.

**Consumer behaviour.** Luxury goods derive value from other people wanting them. A Rolex tells time no better than a Casio. Its value is almost entirely mimetic — you want it because other high-status people want it.

## When to use it

Apply mimetic desire awareness whenever you feel a strong desire — especially a new or sudden one. Ask: "Do I want this because I genuinely want it, or because someone I admire or compete with wants it?" If you can identify the model you're imitating, the desire may be borrowed rather than authentic.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is concluding that all desire is mimetic and therefore inauthentic. Some desires are genuinely intrinsic — hunger, curiosity, the desire for connection. Girard's insight is that many desires we assume are intrinsic are actually imitative. The skill is telling the difference.

## Try it now

Think of something you want right now — a purchase, a career goal, a lifestyle change. Can you identify who you're imitating? If that person didn't want it, would you still want it?
