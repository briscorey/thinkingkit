## How it works

Reciprocity is one of the most powerful and universal social norms. Across virtually all human cultures, when someone does something for you, you feel obligated to return the favour. This works with gifts, concessions, information, trust, and even hostility.

Robert Cialdini identified reciprocity as the first of his six principles of influence because it's automatic, powerful, and surprisingly difficult to resist — even when the initial "gift" was uninvited or unwanted.

The model has two important variants. Direct reciprocity: you help me, I help you. Reciprocal concession: I make a large request you refuse, then make a smaller request — you're more likely to agree to the smaller request because you feel you should reciprocate my concession.

## Case study: How the Hare Krishnas used flowers to raise millions

In the 1970s, the Hare Krishna movement discovered an extraordinarily effective fundraising technique at airports. Before asking for a donation, a Krishna devotee would give the traveller a small gift — a flower, a book, or a pin. The gift was free. No strings attached.

But the reciprocity norm — the deeply ingrained human compulsion to return favours — kicked in immediately. Travellers who received the gift donated at dramatically higher rates than those who were simply asked for money. Many tried to return the flower, but the Krishnas refused to take it back, keeping the reciprocity obligation active.

Robert Cialdini documented this phenomenon in his research on influence. The flowers cost nearly nothing. The donations generated millions. The strategy worked not because people wanted the flowers, but because the act of receiving triggered an automatic, unconscious obligation to give something back. Reciprocity operates below conscious awareness and across every human culture ever studied.

## Real-world examples

**Negotiations.** Starting with a higher ask and then conceding (anchoring + reciprocal concession) is one of the most effective negotiation techniques, precisely because the concession triggers a reciprocal urge to concede in return.

**Networking.** People who give before they ask — sharing resources, making introductions, offering help without strings — build networks that pay dividends for years. The reciprocity is not transactional; it's reputational.

**Management.** Leaders who invest genuine time and energy in their team members' development receive loyalty, effort, and honest feedback in return. Reciprocity works with attention and care, not just material rewards.

## When to use it

Use reciprocity ethically by going first — give before you ask, help before you need help, share before you request. Use it diagnostically: when someone is being unusually generous or accommodating, ask what reciprocity they might be expecting. Use it defensively: recognise when an uninvited gift is designed to trigger a reciprocal obligation you didn't agree to.

## Common mistakes

The ethical line is important. Using reciprocity to manipulate — giving small gifts to create disproportionate obligations — is manipulative and eventually destroys trust. Genuine reciprocity builds relationships; manipulative reciprocity exploits them. The second mistake is expecting immediate reciprocity. The most powerful reciprocity operates on long time horizons.

## Try it now

Identify three people in your professional network. For each one, think of something genuinely useful you could offer them this week — an introduction, a resource, feedback on their work, a recommendation. Do it without asking for anything in return. Notice what happens over the following weeks.
