## How it works

Once you know something, you can't un-know it — and you can't accurately imagine what it's like to not know it. This makes experts systematically terrible at explaining their expertise to beginners. They skip steps that seem "obvious" (because they've internalised them), use jargon without noticing (because it's their normal vocabulary), and misjudge the difficulty of concepts (because they've forgotten how hard it was to learn them).

## Case study: How Elizabeth Newton's tapping experiment revealed the curse in action

In a 1990 Stanford study, Elizabeth Newton asked participants to tap out well-known songs (like "Happy Birthday") with their finger while listeners tried to identify the tune. Tappers predicted listeners would identify 50% of the songs. The actual success rate was 2.5%.

The tappers were stunned. The songs seemed so obvious — because they could hear the melody in their heads while tapping. The listeners heard only rhythmic thumping. The tappers literally couldn't imagine what it was like to hear only taps without the melody. This twenty-fold overestimate of communication effectiveness is the curse of knowledge operating in real time.

## When to use it

Apply curse of knowledge awareness whenever you're explaining, teaching, or communicating something you understand well. Ask: "What did I find confusing when I first learned this? What would someone with no background need to hear first?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is thinking awareness of the curse eliminates it. It doesn't — even knowing about the curse, experts still overestimate how much beginners understand. The fix is structural: test your explanations on actual beginners and iterate based on their confusion, not your judgment.

## Try it now

Pick something you know well and try to explain it to someone who doesn't. Watch where they get confused. Those confusion points are where the curse of knowledge is hiding from you.
