## How it works

The first piece of information you encounter disproportionately shapes all subsequent judgments.

This model helps you recognise situations where this pattern is at play and adjust your thinking accordingly. Understanding it does not make you immune, but it gives you a framework for catching it in action and making better decisions as a result.

## Case study: How Steve Jobs anchored the iPad's price at the 2010 launch

When Steve Jobs unveiled the iPad in January 2010, market analysts and media had been speculating for months that Apple's tablet would cost around $999 — a number Apple had allowed to circulate without correction.

During his keynote, Jobs put "$999" in enormous text on the screen behind him. He let the audience absorb that number. Then he dramatically revealed the actual starting price: $499. The audience gasped.

The $999 figure was the anchor. By the time Jobs revealed the real price, $499 felt like a steal — even though $499 for a new device category with no proven use case was objectively a significant purchase. The anchoring effect meant that every customer's perception of the iPad's value was shaped not by its intrinsic worth, but by the first number they encountered. Jobs didn't just set a price. He set the cognitive reference point that made the price feel like a bargain.

## When to use it

Apply this model whenever you notice its pattern appearing in your decision-making, your team's behaviour, or the systems around you. It is especially valuable in high-stakes situations where the cost of this error is significant.
