## How it works

The best option within your current constraints may not be the best option overall — sometimes you need to get worse before you can get better.

This model helps you recognise situations where this pattern is at play and adjust your thinking accordingly. Understanding it does not make you immune, but it gives you a framework for catching it in action and making better decisions as a result.

## Case study: How Blockbuster found a local optimum and got stuck there

Blockbuster Video optimised relentlessly within its business model: better store locations, more efficient inventory management, superior customer service. Each improvement made the local optimum — the peak of the "physical video rental" hill — slightly higher.

But the global optimum — digital streaming — was on an entirely different hill. To reach it, Blockbuster would have had to descend from their current peak, cross a valley of lower revenue and uncertainty, and climb a new hill with no guarantee of reaching the top.

Netflix started at the bottom of the streaming hill and climbed it while Blockbuster sat atop the rental hill, unable to justify the descent. By the time Blockbuster launched its own streaming service in 2004, Netflix had already established an insurmountable lead. The lesson: the highest point you can see from where you're standing isn't necessarily the highest point that exists.

## When to use it

Apply this model whenever you notice its pattern appearing in your decision-making, your team's behaviour, or the systems around you. It is especially valuable in high-stakes situations where the cost of this error is significant.
