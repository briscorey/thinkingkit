## How it works

When demand for something exceeds supply, its price (or value) rises. When supply exceeds demand, its price falls. This mechanism operates far beyond traditional markets — it applies to jobs, skills, attention, real estate, ideas, and social status.

The model becomes powerful when you use it to explain phenomena that seem mysterious. Why are some highly skilled professionals underpaid? Because the supply of people with that skill exceeds the demand. Why do mediocre products sometimes command premium prices? Because demand exceeds the supply of alternatives in that specific niche.

## Real-world examples

**Careers.** The supply of people who can write basic Python code has increased enormously. The demand for people who can architect complex distributed systems hasn't kept up with the supply of those who can. Result: coding bootcamp graduates compete on price while systems architects command premiums. Skill value isn't about difficulty — it's about scarcity relative to demand.

**Content.** The supply of generic content has exploded (everyone can write a blog post, record a podcast). The demand for genuinely original thinking hasn't changed much. Result: generic content is nearly worthless while distinctive perspective commands a premium.

**Housing.** In a city where 1,000 new residents arrive monthly but only 200 new homes are built, prices rise regardless of interest rates, regulations, or wishful thinking. The supply-demand imbalance is the fundamental driver.

## When to use it

Apply supply-demand thinking whenever you're setting prices, choosing a career path, deciding what skills to develop, evaluating why something costs what it does, or trying to understand market dynamics. It's especially useful for cutting through moralising about prices ("that's too expensive!") and instead understanding the structural forces at work.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is treating supply and demand as the only factor. Government intervention, network effects, switching costs, information asymmetry, and psychological biases all modify the basic model. Supply and demand is the starting framework, not the complete picture.

## Try it now

Think about your most valuable professional skill. How many other people in your market can do the same thing? Is the supply of that skill growing faster or slower than demand? If supply is growing faster, what adjacent skill could you develop that has better supply-demand dynamics?
