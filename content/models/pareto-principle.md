## How it works

Italian economist Vilfredo Pareto noticed that 80% of Italy's land was owned by 20% of the population. The same pattern appears across an extraordinary range of domains: 80% of sales come from 20% of customers, 80% of bugs come from 20% of code, 80% of results come from 20% of effort.

The exact ratio doesn't matter — it's rarely precisely 80/20. The principle is that inputs and outputs are almost never evenly distributed. A small number of causes produce a disproportionately large share of effects.

The practical implication: identify the vital few inputs that drive most of the results, and concentrate your resources there. Most people spread their effort evenly across all activities when they should be ruthlessly focusing on the highest-leverage 20%.

## Real-world examples

**Business.** A typical business finds that ~20% of customers generate ~80% of revenue. The strategic response: invest disproportionately in serving and retaining those top customers rather than spreading effort evenly across all customers.

**Learning.** When learning a new language, ~1,000 words cover ~85% of everyday conversation. Learning those 1,000 words first (the vital few) produces dramatically more conversational ability than learning 1,000 rare words.

**Time management.** Track your productive output for a week. You'll likely find that 2-3 hours produced the majority of your meaningful work, while the remaining 6+ hours were consumed by low-value activities.

## When to use it

Apply the Pareto Principle when prioritising what to work on, when deciding where to cut (start with the 80% of activities producing only 20% of results), when diagnosing problems (look for the 20% of root causes), and when allocating resources across projects, customers, or features.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is treating 80/20 as a precise mathematical law. It's a pattern, not a rule. Sometimes it's 90/10 or 70/30. The second mistake is ignoring the "trivial many" entirely. The 80% of activities producing 20% of results may include essential maintenance, compliance, or relationship work that can't be eliminated. The third mistake is applying it only once. The Pareto Principle is recursive — within the top 20%, there's another 20% that produces 80% of that subset's results.

## Try it now

List your 10 most significant activities or projects. Rank them by impact. Which 2-3 are producing the lion's share of your results? Now look at the bottom 5-7. Could you reduce, delegate, or eliminate any of them to free up time for the top 2-3?
