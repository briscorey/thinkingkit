## How it works

Edward Lorenz discovered that in chaotic systems, infinitesimal differences in initial conditions produce vastly different outcomes. He found that rounding a weather simulation variable from 0.506127 to 0.506 produced completely different weather patterns after a few simulated days. The name comes from his metaphor: a butterfly flapping its wings in Brazil could theoretically set off a tornado in Texas.

The practical implication isn't that butterflies cause tornadoes. It's that in complex, nonlinear systems, long-term prediction is fundamentally impossible — not because we lack data, but because the system amplifies tiny uncertainties into enormous differences.

## Case study: How a wrong turn in Sarajevo started World War I

On June 28, 1914, the motorcade carrying Archduke Franz Ferdinand took a wrong turn onto Franz Josef Street in Sarajevo. By pure coincidence, the car stopped directly in front of Gavrilo Princip, one of the failed assassins from earlier that morning, who was buying a sandwich. Princip shot the Archduke and his wife at point-blank range.

A driver's wrong turn → an assassination → a chain of alliance activations → a four-year war that killed 20 million people → the Treaty of Versailles → the conditions that created World War II → 70 million more deaths → the Cold War → the modern geopolitical order. A single navigational error by a chauffeur arguably shaped the entire 20th century.

## When to use it

Apply butterfly effect thinking to maintain humility about predictions in complex systems. Ask: "Is this system chaotic enough that small changes could produce radically different outcomes?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is using the butterfly effect to argue that nothing is predictable. Many systems are not chaotic — linear systems, simple systems, and systems with strong negative feedback loops are quite predictable. The butterfly effect applies specifically to nonlinear, sensitive-to-initial-conditions systems.

## Try it now

Think of a major outcome in your life — a career, a relationship, a decision. Trace back the chain of causes. What small, seemingly insignificant event started the chain? If that event hadn't happened, how different would things be?
