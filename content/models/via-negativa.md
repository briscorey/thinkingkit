## How it works

Nassim Taleb draws on an ancient philosophical concept: it's easier and more reliable to know what's wrong than what's right. We can identify what makes us sick more reliably than what makes us healthy. We can identify bad practices more easily than optimal ones.

Via negativa means "the negative way" — improving through subtraction rather than addition. Remove the bad rather than adding the supposedly good. Stop doing harmful things before starting new "beneficial" things.

This works because addition has unpredictable second-order effects (new medications interact with existing conditions, new processes create new bureaucracy, new features create new complexity), while subtraction removes known downsides with more predictable effects.

## Real-world examples

**Health.** The most reliable health improvement isn't adding a supplement or a superfood — it's removing ultra-processed food, excessive alcohol, chronic sleep deprivation, and sedentary behaviour. Removing known harms is more impactful and better-evidenced than adding purported benefits.

**Productivity.** Don't add a new productivity system. Remove the three biggest time-wasters in your day. The net effect is usually larger and more sustainable.

**Product design.** Great products are often defined not by what they include but by what they leave out. Removing features that confuse users often improves the product more than adding new ones.

## When to use it

Apply via negativa when you feel the urge to add something new to fix a problem. Ask first: "What could I remove?" When you're overwhelmed by options for improvement, start with the most obviously harmful things in the current situation. When a system is too complex, simplify by subtraction before adding new complexity.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is treating via negativa as absolutist — never add anything. It's a heuristic: subtraction is often higher-leverage than addition, but sometimes addition is necessary. The second mistake is only removing easy, pleasant things. The most impactful removals are often the hardest: the relationship that drains you, the project that's going nowhere, the comfortable habit that's actually harmful.

## Try it now

List five things in your life or work that you suspect are harmful, wasteful, or unnecessary. Pick the one that's most clearly negative. What would happen if you simply stopped? What would you gain by subtraction?
