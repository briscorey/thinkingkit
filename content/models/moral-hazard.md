## How it works

Moral hazard occurs when someone takes more risk because they don't bear the full consequences of that risk. Insurance is the classic case: once you're insured against theft, you might be less careful about locking your door. The insurance doesn't make you dishonest — it changes your incentive structure.

The pattern appears whenever risk and consequences are separated. Employees spending company money are less careful than spending their own. Governments bailing out banks create moral hazard — banks take bigger risks knowing they'll be rescued if things go wrong.

## Real-world examples

**Banking.** "Too big to fail" creates massive moral hazard. Banks that know they'll be bailed out take risks they'd never take with their own money. The profits are private; the catastrophic losses are socialised.

**Rental properties.** Tenants typically maintain a rental less carefully than they would their own home. The cost of wear and tear falls on the landlord, not the tenant.

## When to use it

Look for moral hazard whenever someone bears the benefit of risk but not the full cost. Ask: "If this person had to personally absorb the downside, would they behave differently?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is treating moral hazard as a moral failing. It's not about bad character — it's about incentive structures. Good people behave differently when they're insulated from consequences. The solution is structural (aligning incentives) not moral (asking people to be more careful).

## Try it now

Identify a situation where you or someone else takes risks without bearing the full downside. How would behaviour change if the risk-taker had full skin in the game?
