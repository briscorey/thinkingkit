## How it works

A thought experiment follows a simple structure. First, define a hypothetical scenario with clear parameters. Second, apply logical reasoning to trace the consequences. Third, see if the conclusions reveal something useful about the real world.

The power is in controlled variation. By changing one variable in an imaginary scenario while holding everything else constant, you can isolate the effect of that variable — something that's often impossible in the messy real world.

Einstein imagined chasing a beam of light. What would it look like if you caught up to it? The logical consequences of that simple scenario led to special relativity. You don't need to revolutionise physics — but the same structure works for everyday decisions.

## Real-world examples

**Career decisions.** Thought experiment: "If I knew with certainty that this company would go bankrupt in two years, would I still take this job for the experience alone?" This isolates whether you value the role itself or just the stability.

**Product design.** "If our product were free, would anyone use it?" This isolates whether you're solving a real problem or just benefiting from a lack of alternatives. If the answer is no, you don't have product-market fit — you have pricing-market fit.

**Ethics.** The trolley problem is a thought experiment that isolates the tension between action and inaction in moral reasoning. You don't need a real trolley to learn something about how you think about ethical trade-offs.

## When to use it

Use thought experiments when real-world testing is expensive, slow, dangerous, or impossible, when you need to isolate a single variable from a complex situation, when you want to stress-test a belief by exploring its logical consequences, and when you're stuck and need a fresh angle on a familiar problem.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is confusing the thought experiment with reality. A thought experiment reveals the consequences of your assumptions — if your assumptions are wrong, the conclusions are worthless. The second mistake is making the scenario too complex. The best thought experiments are simple: one change, traced to its logical conclusion.

## Try it now

Pick a decision you're weighing. Create a thought experiment by asking: "If I knew with certainty that [one key uncertainty] would resolve as [specific outcome], what would I do?" Try it with the opposite outcome. Does the answer change? If not, that uncertainty doesn't actually matter for your decision.
