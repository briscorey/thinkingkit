## How it works

A strawman argument weakens an opponent's position to make it easy to attack. A steelman argument strengthens an opponent's position to ensure you're engaging with the best version of their reasoning. Strawmanning wins arguments. Steelmanning wins truth.

The practical test: could the person you're arguing against recognise your representation of their position as fair? If they'd say "yes, that's what I believe," you've steelmanned. If they'd say "that's not what I said at all," you've strawmanned.

## Case study: How Daniel Dennett's steelmanning rules improved philosophical debate

Philosopher Daniel Dennett proposed four rules for criticising someone's argument. First, re-express their position so clearly and fairly that they say "Thanks, I wish I'd put it that way." Second, list any points of agreement. Third, mention anything you learned from their position. Only then — fourth — are you permitted to say a word of criticism.

Dennett's rules force steelmanning before criticism. Philosophers who adopted this approach reported that it dramatically improved the quality of their debates — not because it silenced disagreement, but because it forced each side to truly understand the other before attacking. Most "disagreements" turned out to be misunderstandings that dissolved under the steelmanning requirement.

## When to use it

Apply steelmanning in any disagreement that matters. Before criticising, ask: "Have I represented their position so well that they would agree with my summary?"

## Try it now

Think of someone whose position you disagree with. Write the strongest possible version of their argument — the version they would be proud of. Then try to defeat that version. If you can, your disagreement is genuine. If you can't, you might be wrong.
