## How it works

A feedback loop exists whenever the output of a process feeds back as input to that same process.

**Reinforcing (positive) loops** amplify change. They create exponential growth or collapse. A bank run is a positive feedback loop: a few people withdraw money → others see this and worry → more people withdraw → the bank runs out of cash. Compound interest works the same way in the opposite direction: earnings generate more earnings.

**Balancing (negative) loops** resist change. They pull a system back toward equilibrium. A thermostat is a negative loop: temperature rises above the target → the AC turns on → temperature drops → the AC turns off. Your body uses negative loops to maintain stable temperature, blood sugar, and heart rate.

The key insight from Donella Meadows: most people see events ("the stock price dropped"). Systems thinkers see the loops that produced those events. When you can see the loops, you can find the leverage points — the places where a small intervention changes the system's behaviour.

## Real-world examples

**Social media engagement.** Reinforcing loop: user posts content → gets likes → feels rewarded → posts more → gets more likes. This loop drives addictive usage patterns. The balancing loop: user posts too much → followers unfollow → reach drops → posting feels less rewarding → user posts less.

**Learning.** Reinforcing loop: you study a topic → you understand more → understanding makes it interesting → you study more → you understand more. The opposite is also a reinforcing loop: you struggle → it feels frustrating → you avoid studying → you fall further behind → it's even more frustrating.

## When to use it

Look for feedback loops whenever you see a trend accelerating (ask: what's the reinforcing loop?), whenever a system resists your attempts to change it (ask: what's the balancing loop?), when planning interventions (ask: will this trigger reinforcing or balancing dynamics?), and when you want to build sustainable habits (design reinforcing loops) or break bad ones (interrupt them).

## Common mistakes

The most common mistake is seeing only reinforcing loops and ignoring the balancing forces. Every exponential trend eventually hits a balancing loop — growth can't continue forever. The second mistake is intervening in the wrong part of the loop. Pushing harder against a balancing loop just increases the resistance. The leverage is in changing the loop's structure, not its speed.

## Try it now

Pick a habit you want to build or break. Map the feedback loop: what triggers the behaviour, what reward reinforces it, and what makes it self-sustaining? If you want to build the habit, strengthen the reinforcing loop. If you want to break it, interrupt it at the weakest link.
