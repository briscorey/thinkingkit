## How it works

Tiago Forte's "Building a Second Brain" methodology treats your note-taking system as an extension of your biological memory. Instead of trying to remember everything, you capture, organise, distill, and express information through an external system — freeing your brain to do what it does best: think, connect, and create.

The core framework is PARA: organise everything into Projects (active work), Areas (ongoing responsibilities), Resources (topics of interest), and Archives (completed or inactive items). The key insight: organise by actionability, not by topic.

## Case study: How Vannevar Bush predicted the second brain in 1945

In his 1945 Atlantic article "As We May Think," Vannevar Bush described the "memex" — a theoretical device that would store and cross-reference all of a person's books, records, and communications, with the ability to create "trails" linking related items.

Bush described almost exactly what modern note-taking tools (Notion, Obsidian, Roam) provide today: a personal knowledge base with bidirectional links, searchable archives, and the ability to connect ideas across domains. His core insight — that the future of thinking would depend on external cognitive tools, not just biological memory — was decades ahead of its time and has been fully vindicated by the personal knowledge management revolution.

## When to use it

Build a second brain whenever you're overwhelmed by information, repeatedly re-researching things you've already learned, or struggling to connect ideas across domains. The system works best when you're consistently capturing, not just when you remember to.

## Try it now

What's one idea, insight, or piece of information you learned this week that you'll probably forget? Write it down in a system you'll actually check. That's the first entry in your second brain.
