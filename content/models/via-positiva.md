## How it works

Via Negativa (the negative way) means improving by removing what's harmful rather than adding what might be beneficial. Via Positiva (the positive way) means improving by adding something new. Nassim Taleb argues that Via Negativa is generally more reliable than Via Positiva — it's easier to identify what's clearly harmful than to predict what addition will help.

Removing a bad habit is more reliable than adding a good one. Eliminating toxic food is more impactful than adding supplements. Cutting unnecessary meetings is more productive than adding productivity tools.

The principle connects to Occam's Razor (prefer simplicity), First Principles (strip away assumptions), and the Lindy Effect (what has survived is more trustworthy than what's new).

## Real-world examples

**Health.** Eliminating processed food, excessive alcohol, and sleep deprivation produces far more reliable health improvement than adding any supplement, superfood, or biohack.

**Productivity.** Eliminating your three biggest time-wasters produces more output than adding any new productivity system or tool.

## When to use it

When you want to improve something, ask Via Negativa first: "What could I remove that's causing harm or drag?" Only after you've subtracted the obvious negatives should you look for positives to add.

## Common mistakes

The mistake is applying Via Negativa to everything. Some situations genuinely require addition — you can't remove your way to a new skill, a new relationship, or a new product. Via Negativa is most powerful for optimisation and maintenance, not creation.

## Try it now

Pick one area you want to improve. Before adding anything new, list three things you could remove that are causing friction, waste, or harm. Start there.
