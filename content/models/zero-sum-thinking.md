## How it works

Zero-sum thinking assumes that every interaction has a fixed pie — that one person's gain must be another's loss. Sometimes this is true: in a poker game, every dollar you win comes from another player. But in many situations, the pie can grow. Trade, cooperation, innovation, and technology all create positive-sum games where everyone can gain.

The most consequential thinking error is applying zero-sum logic to positive-sum situations — treating negotiations as battles, viewing colleagues as competitors, or opposing trade because "if they gain, we lose."

## Case study: How post-WWII trade agreements turned enemies into partners

After World War II, the U.S. could have treated international trade as zero-sum — keeping Germany and Japan economically weak to prevent future threats. Instead, the Marshall Plan and GATT trade agreements deliberately helped former enemies rebuild their economies.

Zero-sum thinkers predicted disaster: why strengthen your competitors? But trade is positive-sum. U.S. exports to Europe tripled. German and Japanese reconstruction created massive demand for American goods. By 1960, all participants were significantly wealthier than in 1945. The "competitors" the U.S. helped rebuild became its largest trading partners and closest allies.

The positive-sum approach — helping others succeed because their success creates markets, stability, and innovation — generated more wealth for everyone than any zero-sum strategy could have.

## When to use it

Apply zero-sum awareness whenever you're in a conflict or negotiation. Ask: "Is this actually zero-sum, or am I treating an expandable pie as fixed?" In most relationships, businesses, and negotiations, the pie can grow.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is assuming everything is positive-sum. Some situations genuinely are zero-sum: elections, sports, competitive bidding. The skill is recognising which type of game you're actually playing.

## Try it now

Think of a current conflict or negotiation in your life. Are you treating it as zero-sum? Is there a way to expand the pie so both sides gain? What would you have to offer or change to make it positive-sum?
