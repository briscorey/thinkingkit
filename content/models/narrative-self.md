## How it works

Identity-protective cognition means we evaluate evidence not by its quality but by whether accepting it would threaten the identity of our group. Dan Kahan's research shows that people with high scientific literacy are actually more polarised on politically charged scientific topics — because they use their intelligence to construct better arguments for their side, not to find the truth.

The mechanism is subtle: we don't consciously decide to reject evidence. We experience the threatening evidence as less credible, less relevant, or less well-conducted. Our identity filters the evidence before our analytical mind gets to evaluate it.

## Case study: How scientific literacy increases polarisation on climate change

Kahan's studies found that people with the highest science comprehension scores showed the largest partisan gap on climate change beliefs. Among low-science-comprehension participants, there was modest disagreement. Among high-science-comprehension participants, the disagreement was extreme.

The scientifically literate weren't more objective — they were better at constructing arguments that protected their group's position. Liberal scientists marshalled evidence for concern. Conservative scientists marshalled evidence for scepticism. Both groups used genuine scientific reasoning in service of identity protection.

## When to use it

Apply identity-protective cognition awareness whenever you find yourself evaluating evidence on a topic where your group has a strong position. Ask: "Am I evaluating this evidence on its merits, or through the lens of my group identity?"

## Try it now

Pick a topic where your social group has a strong consensus view. Now find the strongest evidence against that view. Notice your emotional response. Is the evidence genuinely weak, or does it just feel threatening?
