## How it works

Stephen Covey's Circle of Influence model distinguishes between things you can directly control (your effort, attitude, choices), things you can influence (your team's morale, your manager's decisions, your market positioning), and things you can neither control nor influence (the weather, the economy, other people's feelings, government policy).

Most people spend disproportionate time and emotional energy worrying about things outside their circle of influence. This is worse than unproductive — it's actively draining, because effort spent on uncontrollable things produces zero results while depleting the energy you could spend on things within your control.

## Real-world examples

**Career.** You can't control whether you get the promotion (outside your circle). You can control the quality of your work, the relationships you build, and how you prepare for the interview (inside your circle).

**Health.** You can't control your genetic predispositions (outside). You can control your sleep, nutrition, and exercise (inside). Focus there.

## When to use it

Whenever you feel stressed, anxious, or frustrated, audit: "Is the source of my stress inside or outside my circle of influence?" If outside, redirect your energy to what you can actually affect.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is assuming your circle of influence is smaller than it actually is. Many things that feel uncontrollable can actually be influenced with the right approach. The second mistake is using this as an excuse for passivity — "I can't control it, so I won't try." Some things outside your direct control are still worth influencing.

## Try it now

Write down the three things causing you the most stress right now. For each, ask: "Is this inside my circle of influence?" If yes, define one action you can take this week. If no, practice letting it go and redirecting that energy to something you can affect.
