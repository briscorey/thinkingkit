## How it works

Daniel Kahneman discovered that when people evaluate past experiences, they don't calculate the average pleasantness or total duration. They judge the experience based on two moments: the peak (most intense point) and the end. Everything else is largely ignored by memory.

This means a painful medical procedure that ends with a period of gradually decreasing pain is remembered as less painful than a shorter procedure that ends at peak pain — even though the longer procedure involved more total pain. Memory doesn't track totals. It tracks peaks and endings.

## Case study: How Disney engineers endings that override hours of waiting in line

Disney theme parks are masterfully designed around the peak-end rule. Visitors wait in long lines (often 60-90 minutes), experience brief rides (2-4 minutes), and consistently rate the experience as magical. The trick: Disney engineers the peak (the ride's climax) and the ending (the exit experience, including a themed gift shop and photo opportunity) to be overwhelmingly positive.

The hours of waiting, which dominate the actual experience by time, barely register in memory because they contain neither the peak nor the end. Disney doesn't need to fix the wait — they need to nail the peak and the ending. And they do, consistently, which is why guests return despite spending most of their day standing in lines.

## When to use it

Apply the peak-end rule when designing experiences, presentations, conversations, or any sequence of events where you want to be remembered positively. Invest disproportionately in the most intense moment and the final moment.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is neglecting the experience to focus only on the peak and end. While memory follows the peak-end rule, the in-the-moment experience still matters for wellbeing. A miserable experience with a great ending is remembered well but lived poorly.

## Try it now

Think of the last presentation you gave, meeting you ran, or event you hosted. How did it end? Was the ending deliberately designed, or did it just fizzle out? How could you engineer a stronger ending next time?
