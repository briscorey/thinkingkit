## How it works

Seek decisions where the potential upside vastly outweighs the potential downside — even if success is unlikely.

This model helps you recognise situations where this pattern is at play and adjust your thinking accordingly. Understanding it does not make you immune, but it gives you a framework for catching it in action and making better decisions as a result.

## Case study: How Nassim Taleb structured the trade that defined his career

In the years before the 2008 financial crisis, most investors saw the growing housing market and loaded up on mortgage-backed securities. The expected return was positive. The conventional risk models said the probability of a crash was negligible.

Taleb and his fund took the opposite position — but not a symmetrical one. They didn't bet everything on a crash. They structured asymmetric trades: buying cheap, far-out-of-the-money options that would expire worthless in normal conditions but pay off enormously in a crisis. The most they could lose was the small premium paid for the options. The most they could gain was theoretically unlimited.

When the crisis hit, the asymmetric payoff was extraordinary. The key wasn't predicting the crash — it was structuring the trade so that being wrong cost little and being right paid enormously. Asymmetric risk isn't about having better predictions. It's about having better payoff structures.

## When to use it

Apply this model whenever you notice its pattern appearing in your decision-making, your team's behaviour, or the systems around you. It is especially valuable in high-stakes situations where the cost of this error is significant.
