## How it works

The scientific method is the most reliable process humans have developed for separating truth from opinion. Its power comes from a simple structure: form a hypothesis, design a test that could prove it wrong, run the test, observe what happens, and update your beliefs accordingly.

The critical element is falsifiability — you must be able to specify what evidence would disprove your hypothesis. A theory that explains everything explains nothing. If no possible observation could prove you wrong, you're not doing science — you're defending a belief.

Applied to everyday thinking, this means treating your beliefs as hypotheses rather than conclusions, actively seeking disconfirming evidence, and updating your views when the evidence demands it.

## Real-world examples

**Business decisions.** Instead of "our customers want feature X" (belief), reframe as: "If we build feature X, usage will increase by 20% within 30 days" (testable hypothesis). Build it. Measure. Update.

**Personal development.** Instead of "meditation will reduce my anxiety" (belief), try: "If I meditate for 10 minutes daily for 30 days, my weekly anxiety score will decrease" (testable). Track it. See what happens.

## When to use it

Apply the scientific method whenever you're acting on a belief that hasn't been tested. The more important the decision, the more valuable it is to test rather than assume.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is confirmation bias in testing — designing experiments that can only confirm your hypothesis, not disconfirm it. The second mistake is treating one test as proof. Single experiments establish evidence, not certainty. Replication matters.

## Try it now

Pick one belief that drives a decision in your work or life. Rewrite it as a testable hypothesis with a specific prediction and timeframe. What would you need to observe to consider it disproven?
