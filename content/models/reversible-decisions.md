## How it works

Jeff Bezos categorises decisions as Type 1 (one-way doors, irreversible) and Type 2 (two-way doors, reversible). Most decisions are Type 2 — if they don't work out, you can reverse them with minimal cost. But organisations often treat every decision as Type 1, applying heavyweight deliberation to lightweight choices.

The framework prescribes different processes: Type 1 decisions deserve careful analysis, broad input, and senior review. Type 2 decisions should be made quickly by individuals or small teams and reversed if wrong. Applying Type 1 process to Type 2 decisions creates slowness. Applying Type 2 speed to Type 1 decisions creates recklessness.

## Case study: How Amazon's "disagree and commit" culture ships faster than competitors

When Amazon teams can't reach consensus on a Type 2 decision, Bezos encourages "disagree and commit" — voice your disagreement clearly, then commit fully to the decision once it's made. Don't sabotage, don't drag your feet, don't say "I told you so" if it fails.

This approach lets Amazon make decisions in hours that competitors deliberate for weeks. Most of these decisions are reversible — if the feature doesn't work, kill it. If the pricing is wrong, change it. The cost of being wrong on a Type 2 decision is almost always less than the cost of deciding slowly.

## When to use it

Before every decision, ask: "If this turns out wrong, how hard is it to reverse?" For easily reversible decisions, decide fast and iterate. For irreversible decisions, slow down and get it right.

Look at your current decision queue. Label each as Type 1 or Type 2. How many Type 2 decisions are you treating as Type 1? Make those right now.
