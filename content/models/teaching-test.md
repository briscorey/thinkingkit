## How it works

Richard Feynman's learning technique has four steps. First, choose a concept you want to understand. Second, explain it as if you're teaching it to someone with no background knowledge — use simple language, no jargon, and concrete examples. Third, identify the gaps — the places where your explanation breaks down, where you resort to jargon, or where you can't find a simple analogy. Fourth, go back to the source material and fill those gaps, then try the explanation again.

The power of the technique is that teaching forces a different kind of understanding than reading or listening. You can read something and feel you understand it. But when you try to explain it simply, the gaps in your understanding become immediately visible.

## Case study: How Richard Feynman explained the Challenger disaster to the nation with a glass of ice water

After the 1986 Challenger disaster, Richard Feynman served on the presidential commission investigating the cause. The technical issue — O-ring failure due to cold temperature — was buried in engineering jargon and bureaucratic reports that few people could understand.

At a televised hearing, Feynman took a piece of O-ring rubber, dropped it into a glass of ice water, and squeezed it with a C-clamp. When he removed the clamp, the rubber didn't spring back — cold had made it rigid. In ten seconds, without a single technical term, he demonstrated the cause of a disaster that had killed seven people. A glass of ice water explained what thousands of pages of engineering reports couldn't.

## When to use it

Use the Feynman Technique whenever you want to genuinely understand something — not just pass a test or sound knowledgeable, but actually comprehend it at a deep level. The technique works for any domain: physics, business strategy, history, cooking.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is confusing the ability to use jargon with genuine understanding. If you can only explain something in technical terms, you may be pattern-matching rather than understanding. The second mistake is giving up when the explanation gets hard — the difficulty is the point. That's where learning happens.

## Try it now

Pick a concept you think you understand well. Set a timer for two minutes and explain it out loud as if you're talking to a curious 12-year-old. Where did you stumble? Where did you reach for jargon? Those are the gaps in your understanding.
