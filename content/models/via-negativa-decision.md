## How it works

When facing a decision about how to improve something, the instinct is Via Positiva — add something new. A better tool, a new process, an additional team member. Via Negativa asks the opposite: what could you remove to improve the situation?

Subtraction is underrated because addition feels productive while removal feels like giving up. But removing friction, waste, complexity, and distraction often produces more improvement than any addition could.

## Case study: How In-N-Out Burger dominated fast food by offering fewer options

While McDonald's expanded to a menu of 150+ items, In-N-Out Burger maintained essentially the same menu since 1948: hamburger, cheeseburger, double-double, fries, drinks, shakes. No salads, no breakfast, no dozens of limited-time offers.

The constrained menu allows In-N-Out to maintain exceptional quality on every item, train employees faster, reduce errors, minimise waste, and keep prices low. Their customer satisfaction scores consistently rank at or near the top of all fast food chains. The strategy of removing options — Via Negativa — produced a better result than the strategy of endlessly adding them.

## When to use it

Before adding anything new to your life, work, or systems, ask the Via Negativa question first: "What could I remove that would improve things more than anything I could add?"

## Try it now

List three things you could remove from your work this week — a meeting, a report, a process step, a tool. Would the output suffer? If not, remove them. The freed capacity is your improvement.
