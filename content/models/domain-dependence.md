## How it works

Domain dependence is the tendency to compartmentalise knowledge and fail to transfer insights from one area to another, even when the underlying principle is identical. Someone might understand compound interest in finance but fail to recognise the same exponential growth dynamic in their skills, relationships, or health habits.

Nassim Taleb describes this as one of the most common failures of sophisticated thinkers: they understand a concept perfectly in one context and fail to apply it in another, even when a simple analogy would reveal the connection.

The antidote is cross-domain thinking — the deliberate practice of asking "where else does this pattern appear?"

## Real-world examples

**Evolution and business.** Natural selection and market competition follow the same dynamics (variation, selection, retention), yet biology professors rarely apply evolutionary thinking to their department's strategy, and business strategists rarely study ecology.

**Engineering and relationships.** An engineer who understands that systems need slack (redundancy, buffer time, margin of error) to function well may nonetheless pack their personal schedule to 100% capacity, leaving no slack for the unexpected.

## When to use it

Apply domain independence whenever you learn a powerful concept in one field. Ask: "Where else does this pattern show up? What other problems share this structure?" The most creative solutions often come from importing a well-understood framework from one domain into a domain where it's unfamiliar.

## Common mistakes

The mistake is forcing analogies where they don't apply. Not every pattern transfers. The skill is recognising which structural features are shared across domains and which are domain-specific.

## Try it now

Take a concept you understand deeply in your professional field. Ask: "Does this same dynamic appear in my health, relationships, finances, or learning?" Write down at least two cross-domain applications.
