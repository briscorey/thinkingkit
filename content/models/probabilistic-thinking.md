## How it works

Most people think in binaries: this will work or it won't. The market will go up or down. I'll get the job or I won't. This feels natural but it's dangerously imprecise.

Probabilistic thinking replaces "yes or no" with "how likely." Instead of "Will this project succeed?" you ask "What's the probability of success, and what are the main scenarios?" This sounds abstract but it changes behaviour in concrete ways.

Philip Tetlock's research on forecasting found that the best predictors — "superforecasters" — share one defining trait: they think in probabilities and update them constantly as new information arrives. They say "there's a 70% chance of X" rather than "X will happen," and they track whether their 70% predictions come true roughly 70% of the time.

The practice has three steps. First, estimate a probability for your prediction (even a rough one). Second, identify what evidence would make you update that probability up or down. Third, when new evidence appears, actually update.

## Real-world examples

**Job searching.** Instead of "I'll get this job," think "I'd estimate a 15% chance of getting an offer — it's competitive and I'm slightly underqualified, but my portfolio is strong." This framing lets you apply to more positions without the emotional whiplash of treating each as all-or-nothing.

**Investing.** Instead of "This stock will go up," think "There's a 60% chance it rises, a 25% chance it trades sideways, and a 15% chance it drops significantly." Now you can size your position appropriately rather than going all-in on a binary bet.

## When to use it

Use probabilistic thinking for any decision under uncertainty (which is most decisions), when you catch yourself saying "definitely," "never," or "always" about the future, when you need to compare options with different risk profiles, and when you want to improve your judgment over time by tracking your accuracy.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is false precision. Saying "there's a 73.2% chance" implies more accuracy than you have. Rough ranges (60-80%) are more honest. The second mistake is failing to update. A probability estimate is only useful if you revise it when new evidence arrives. The third mistake is anchoring on your first estimate — being willing to move is the whole point.

## Try it now

Pick three predictions you hold about the next month — a work outcome, a personal goal, something in the news. Assign each a probability between 0% and 100%. Write them down. At the end of the month, check: were your 80% predictions right about 80% of the time?
