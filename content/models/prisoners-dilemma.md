## How it works

Two suspects are arrested and interrogated separately. Each can cooperate (stay silent) or defect (betray the other). If both cooperate, both get light sentences. If both defect, both get heavy sentences. If one defects while the other cooperates, the defector goes free and the cooperator gets the heaviest sentence.

The dilemma: regardless of what the other person does, defecting is individually rational. But if both follow this logic, both end up worse off than if they'd cooperated. Individual rationality leads to collective irrationality.

## Case study: How Robert Axelrod's tournament proved that "nice" strategies win

In 1980, political scientist Robert Axelrod invited game theorists to submit strategies for a repeated Prisoner's Dilemma tournament. Sophisticated strategies competed: always defect, random, vindictive, manipulative.

The winner was the simplest strategy submitted: Tit for Tat, by Anatol Rapoport. It cooperates on the first move, then copies whatever the opponent did on the previous move. It's "nice" (never defects first), retaliatory (punishes defection immediately), forgiving (returns to cooperation after the opponent does), and clear (its behaviour is predictable).

In the repeated version of the dilemma, cooperation becomes rational because the relationship continues. Tit for Tat's success showed that in ongoing relationships, niceness, retaliation, forgiveness, and clarity outperform both naive cooperation and cynical defection.

## When to use it

Apply Prisoner's Dilemma thinking in any situation with a cooperation/defection tradeoff. Ask: "Is this a one-shot interaction or an ongoing relationship? In ongoing relationships, cooperative strategies with clear consequences for betrayal tend to win."

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is treating a repeated game like a one-shot game. In one-shot interactions, defection may be rational. In ongoing relationships, building a reputation for cooperation with clear retaliation is superior.

## Try it now

Think of a relationship — business or personal — where there's tension between competing and cooperating. Is this a one-shot or repeated interaction? How would a Tit-for-Tat approach change your strategy?
