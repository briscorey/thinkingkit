## How it works

The clustering illusion is our tendency to see meaningful patterns in random data. Thomas Gilovich showed that basketball fans firmly believe in the "hot hand" — that a player who has made several shots in a row is more likely to make the next one. Statistical analysis of actual shooting data showed no hot hand effect. The "streaks" fans saw were exactly what you'd expect from random sequences.

The human brain is a pattern-detection machine — it evolved to find patterns because pattern recognition conferred survival advantages. But the same machinery that spots a predator hiding in foliage also "spots" patterns in random noise.

## Case study: How the London Blitz bombing pattern convinced Londoners that the Germans had spies

During the 1940 Blitz, Londoners noticed that some neighbourhoods were bombed repeatedly while others were spared. The pattern seemed too systematic to be random — surely the Germans had intelligence about which areas to target. A detailed statistical analysis by R.D. Clarke revealed that the bombing pattern was perfectly consistent with random distribution. The clusters were exactly what probability theory predicted from randomly scattered hits. There was no targeting pattern. Londoners' pattern-seeking brains had imposed meaning on randomness.

## When to use it

Apply clustering illusion awareness whenever you notice a "pattern" in data. Ask: "Could this pattern arise by chance? How does this compare to what random data would look like?"

## Try it now

Flip a coin 20 times and write down the sequence. You'll almost certainly see "streaks" of 3-4 in a row. Are they meaningful? No — they're what randomness looks like in small samples.
