## How it works

We judge the likelihood of events by how easily examples come to mind — not by actual frequency.

This model helps you recognise situations where this pattern is at play and adjust your thinking accordingly. Understanding it does not make you immune, but it gives you a framework for catching it in action and making better decisions as a result.

## Case study: How shark attacks distort beach safety policy

After the 2001 "Summer of the Shark" — a media frenzy triggered by a series of highly publicised shark attacks in Florida — beach attendance dropped significantly and several coastal communities invested millions in shark prevention measures.

The reality: shark attacks in 2001 were actually lower than the five-year average. The number hadn't increased. The media coverage had. A handful of vivid, terrifying stories created an availability cascade — the more coverage there was, the more people feared sharks, the more media covered the fear, and the cycle reinforced itself.

Meanwhile, the genuinely dangerous activity at the beach — drowning in rip currents — killed roughly 100 Americans per year with almost zero media coverage. Resources were being allocated to the vivid risk (sharks: ~1 death per year) instead of the statistically significant risk (drowning: ~100 deaths per year) because shark attacks are memorable and rip currents are not.

## When to use it

Apply this model whenever you notice its pattern appearing in your decision-making, your team's behaviour, or the systems around you. It is especially valuable in high-stakes situations where the cost of this error is significant.
