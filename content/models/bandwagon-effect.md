## How it works

The bandwagon effect is the tendency to adopt beliefs, styles, and behaviours because you see many others doing so. The more people who hold a view or take an action, the more likely others are to follow — not because the view has merit, but because popularity substitutes for independent evaluation.

The effect creates self-reinforcing cycles: as more people join, the behaviour appears more validated, attracting more joiners. This can drive both positive outcomes (adoption of genuinely good ideas) and catastrophic ones (speculative bubbles, mass hysteria, harmful fads).

## Case study: How the Dutch tulip mania of 1637 created history's first speculative bubble

In the 1630s, tulip bulb prices in the Netherlands rose astronomically. At the peak, a single Semper Augustus bulb sold for more than ten times the annual income of a skilled craftsman. People who had no interest in horticulture began buying bulbs purely because everyone else was buying them and prices kept rising.

The bandwagon effect was the engine: each buyer's purchase raised prices, which attracted more buyers, which raised prices further. When the bubble burst in February 1637, prices collapsed by over 90% in weeks. The bulbs hadn't changed. Only the bandwagon had stopped.

## When to use it

Apply bandwagon awareness whenever you're considering doing something primarily because "everyone's doing it." Ask: "Would I make this same choice if nobody else was doing it?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is assuming the bandwagon is always wrong. Sometimes the crowd is right — popular things are often popular for good reasons. The skill is distinguishing between popularity-as-evidence and popularity-as-the-only-reason.

## Try it now

Identify one belief or behaviour you've adopted recently. Did you evaluate it independently, or did you adopt it primarily because many people around you hold it? If the latter, what evidence would you need to confirm it's actually correct?
