## How it works

Some decisions can be reversed cheaply; others can't. Jeff Bezos calls these "two-way doors" and "one-way doors." Two-way doors (reversible decisions) deserve fast action — if you're wrong, you walk back through. One-way doors (irreversible decisions) deserve careful deliberation because there's no going back.

The practical implication: match your decision-making effort to the reversibility of the decision. Most people apply the same level of deliberation to all decisions, which means they're too slow on reversible ones and sometimes too fast on irreversible ones.

## Real-world examples

**Career.** Taking a new job in the same industry is mostly reversible — you can leave and find another. Accepting a two-year assignment in a remote location with no career alternatives is less reversible. The second decision deserves more deliberation.

**Technology.** Choosing a programming language for a weekend project is highly reversible. Choosing a database architecture for a system that will hold millions of records is far less reversible.

## When to use it

Before any significant decision, ask: "Is this a one-way door or a two-way door?" For two-way doors, decide quickly and iterate. For one-way doors, invest the time to get it right.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is treating everything as irreversible, leading to paralysis. Most decisions are more reversible than they feel. The second mistake is assuming something is reversible when the reversal costs are actually prohibitive — technically reversible but practically a one-way door.

## Try it now

List three decisions you're currently facing. For each, ask: "If this turns out wrong, how easily can I reverse it and at what cost?" Spend proportional time on each based on its reversibility.
