## How it works

Richard Thaler's research showed that people value things they own more than identical things they don't own. In a classic experiment, participants who received a coffee mug demanded roughly twice as much to sell it as other participants were willing to pay to buy it. The mug didn't change. Ownership changed its perceived value.

The endowment effect is closely related to loss aversion: selling something you own feels like a loss, and losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasant. So you demand a higher price to compensate for the "loss" of giving up your possession.

## Case study: How the endowment effect keeps people in overpriced homes

Behavioural economists have documented that homeowners consistently overprice their properties relative to market value — by an average of 5-12%. The mechanism: they've lived in the house, made memories there, and customised it to their taste. These personal associations inflate their perception of what the house is worth.

During housing downturns, the endowment effect causes particular damage. Owners who bought at peak prices anchor to their purchase price and refuse to sell at the lower market price, even when holding costs (mortgage, taxes, maintenance) make selling the rational choice. They're paying real money to avoid the psychological pain of realising a loss on "their" house.

## When to use it

Apply endowment effect awareness whenever you're deciding whether to keep or sell something. Ask: "If I didn't already own this, would I buy it at the price I'm asking?" If not, the endowment effect is inflating your valuation.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is concluding that all ownership premiums are irrational. Sometimes personal customisation genuinely increases value to you. The question is whether the premium reflects real personal value or just the psychological pain of letting go.

## Try it now

Pick something you own — a stock, a piece of equipment, a subscription, a project. If you didn't already have it, would you acquire it at its current cost? If the answer is no, the endowment effect may be keeping you invested in something you should release.
