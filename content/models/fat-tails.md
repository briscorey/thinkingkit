## How it works

In a normal (bell curve) distribution, extreme events are vanishingly rare. In a fat-tailed distribution, extreme events are far more common than the bell curve predicts. The "tails" of the distribution — where the rare, extreme outcomes live — are "fat" rather than thin.

Nassim Taleb argues that most consequential real-world phenomena follow fat-tailed distributions: financial returns, earthquake magnitudes, pandemic severity, war casualties, book sales, startup valuations. In these domains, using normal distribution assumptions dramatically underestimates the frequency and severity of extreme events.

## Case study: How the 2008 financial crisis was a "25-sigma event" that happened anyway

Risk models at major banks used normal distributions to estimate the probability of large market movements. Under these models, the daily market moves seen during the 2008 crisis were "25-sigma events" — so improbable that they should occur less than once in the lifetime of the universe.

Yet they happened on multiple consecutive days. The models weren't wrong about the direction — they were wrong about the distribution. Real financial markets have fat tails: extreme moves are orders of magnitude more common than Gaussian models predict. The banks had been using the wrong map for the territory's actual shape.

## When to use it

Apply fat-tail thinking in any domain where extreme events are consequential: finance, technology, natural disasters, pandemics, business outcomes. Ask: "Am I using a bell curve assumption when the real distribution has fat tails?"

## Common mistakes

The mistake is seeing fat tails everywhere. Many phenomena genuinely follow normal distributions (human height, measurement errors, IQ scores). The skill is knowing which domain you're in. A useful heuristic: if you can see clear, scalable extreme outcomes (a book selling 100 million copies, a stock dropping 90%), you're likely in fat-tail territory.

## Try it now

Think about the most consequential risks in your work or investments. Are you estimating their probability using normal distribution intuition? What would change if extreme outcomes were 10-100x more likely than your mental model assumes?
