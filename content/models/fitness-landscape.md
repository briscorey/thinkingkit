## How it works

Imagine all possible strategies as points on a landscape where height represents success. Evolution — and innovation — works by climbing uphill toward peaks. But the landscape has multiple peaks of different heights. If you're on a local peak, every step in any direction goes downhill, even though a higher peak exists elsewhere.

This is the fundamental tension in both biological evolution and business strategy: the process of local optimisation (climbing the nearest hill) can trap you on a suboptimal peak, unable to reach the global optimum without first descending into a valley.

## Case study: How Nokia was trapped on a local peak while Apple found the global optimum

In 2007, Nokia was the world's most successful mobile phone company, sitting atop the feature phone peak. They had optimised everything about feature phone design: durability, battery life, call quality, manufacturing efficiency. Every incremental improvement climbed higher on their local peak.

Apple launched the iPhone from the bottom of a completely different hill — the smartphone peak. Initially, the iPhone was worse than Nokia phones at being a phone (shorter battery life, fragile, expensive). But the smartphone peak was fundamentally higher. Nokia couldn't reach it without first descending from their feature phone peak — abandoning their optimised manufacturing, their established distribution, and their proven design language. They couldn't stomach the descent, and by the time they tried, Apple had climbed too far ahead.

## When to use it

Apply fitness landscape thinking when you feel stuck optimising within a diminishing paradigm. Ask: "Am I climbing a local peak? Is there a higher peak that requires crossing a valley to reach?"

## Try it now

Map your current strategy as a point on a landscape. Are you still climbing (improvements are producing results) or at a peak (improvements are producing diminishing returns)? If at a peak, what might the global optimum look like?
