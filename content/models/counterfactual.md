## How it works

Counterfactual thinking means asking: "What if things had been different?" By imagining alternative histories — different decisions, different circumstances, different timing — you can separate genuine skill from luck, identify the true causes of outcomes, and make better decisions going forward.

There are two types. Upward counterfactuals ("It could have gone better if...") help identify improvements. Downward counterfactuals ("It could have gone worse if...") help appreciate what went right and identify hidden risks that happened not to materialise.

## Real-world examples

**Business success.** A startup succeeds and the founder attributes it to their strategy. Counterfactual: "Would this strategy have worked if we'd launched six months later, or in a different market?" If the answer is no, the success may owe more to timing and luck than to the strategy itself.

**Near misses.** You drive through an intersection just before a truck runs the red light. Counterfactual thinking reveals that your safety was a matter of seconds, not skill. This recalibrates your risk perception.

## When to use it

Apply counterfactual thinking after any significant outcome — success or failure. Ask: "What had to be true for this to happen? Which of those factors were under my control, and which were luck?" This is how you build genuine understanding rather than false confidence.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is using counterfactuals to ruminate rather than learn. "If only I had..." spirals are emotionally draining and unproductive. The productive version is specific: "If factor X had been different, the outcome would have been Y — therefore factor X is the key variable to focus on next time."

## Try it now

Think of a recent success. Now change one factor: different timing, a different person involved, a different market condition. Would it still have succeeded? The factors where changing them changes the outcome are the ones that actually mattered.
