## How it works

The Winner's Curse occurs in competitive situations — auctions, bidding wars, hiring competitions — where the winner tends to have overpaid. The logic: if many informed people are bidding, the one who bids highest likely overestimated the value more than anyone else. Winning is itself evidence of overvaluation.

The more bidders there are, the stronger the curse. With many competitors, the winning bid is increasingly likely to be an outlier on the high end of estimates.

## Real-world examples

**Corporate acquisitions.** Research consistently shows that acquiring companies tend to overpay for targets. The "winning" bid in a competitive acquisition often destroys shareholder value because the premium paid exceeds the actual synergies captured.

**Job offers.** If you're the candidate who asked for the highest salary and still got the offer, consider: were you priced at fair value, or did the company accept your inflated ask because they were desperate?

## When to use it

Apply winner's curse thinking in any competitive bidding situation. Ask: "If I win this, does that mean I valued it more than everyone else — and is that confidence justified?" The more competitors, the more sceptical you should be of your own "winning" valuation.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is avoiding all competition. The winner's curse doesn't mean you should never bid — it means you should factor the curse into your bid. Bid slightly below your estimated value, knowing that if you win, you've likely overestimated.

## Try it now

Think of the last competitive situation you won — an auction, a negotiation, a bidding war. Did you feel great about winning? Now ask: "Did I pay more than the second-highest bidder? If so, how much of that premium was based on genuine value versus competitive emotion?"
