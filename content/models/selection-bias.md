## How it works

Selection bias occurs when the sample you're observing isn't representative of the full population because of how it was selected. You're drawing conclusions from a filtered subset without realising the filter exists.

The most common form: you only see the data that made it through a selection process, not the data that was filtered out. This is closely related to survivorship bias, but broader — any non-random filtering of observations creates selection bias.

## Real-world examples

**Customer feedback.** The customers who contact support are not representative of all customers. They're the ones with the strongest feelings — usually negative. Designing your product based only on support tickets means you're optimising for the loudest minority.

**Research studies.** Studies with positive, interesting results get published. Studies with null results don't. Reading the published literature gives you a systematically biased view of what works.

## When to use it

Apply selection bias awareness whenever you're drawing conclusions from data. Ask: "How was this sample selected? What's missing? Who or what didn't make it into this dataset, and why?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is assuming that large samples eliminate selection bias. A million biased data points are still biased. Size doesn't fix systematic filtering — only understanding the selection mechanism does.

## Try it now

Think of a dataset or set of observations you recently used to draw a conclusion. How was that data collected? What observations might be missing? Would the conclusion change if the missing data were included?
