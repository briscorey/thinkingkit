## How it works

Correlation means two things tend to move together. Causation means one thing actually makes the other happen. These are fundamentally different, but our brains constantly confuse them.

There are three possibilities when two things correlate. A causes B (direct causation). B causes A (reverse causation). Or C causes both A and B (common cause, also called a confounding variable). Correctly distinguishing between these requires controlled experiments, not just observation.

## Case study: How the stork-baby correlation fooled European demographers

For decades, a genuine statistical correlation existed between the number of stork nests in European regions and the local birth rate. More storks, more babies. Some people seriously proposed that storks brought babies.

The common cause was urbanisation. Rural areas had more suitable habitats for storks (chimneys, barns) and higher birth rates (larger families). Urban areas had fewer stork-friendly structures and lower birth rates. Storks didn't cause babies. Rural living conditions caused both more storks and more babies. The correlation was real. The causal inference was absurd.

## When to use it

Apply this distinction every time someone presents a correlation as evidence of causation. Ask three questions: "Could A be causing B?" "Could B be causing A?" "Could something else be causing both?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is dismissing all correlations as meaningless. Correlation isn't causation, but it is evidence — it narrows the search space. A strong, consistent, dose-dependent correlation with a plausible mechanism is suggestive of causation, even before a controlled experiment proves it.

## Try it now

Find a correlation you've recently used to make a decision. Challenge it with all three possibilities: could the causation run the other direction? Could a third factor explain both?
