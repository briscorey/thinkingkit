## How it works

The IKEA Effect, named by Michael Norton, Daniel Mochon, and Dan Ariely, describes our tendency to overvalue things we've had a hand in creating. Participants in their studies valued self-assembled IKEA furniture 63% more than identical pre-assembled furniture. The effort invested created attachment that inflated perceived value.

The effect extends beyond furniture: code you've written, meals you've cooked, strategies you've developed, and ideas you've contributed to all receive an ownership premium in your evaluation — regardless of their objective quality.

## Case study: How Betty Crocker increased cake mix sales by making bakers add an egg

In the 1950s, Betty Crocker's instant cake mix was a commercial disappointment despite producing consistently good cakes. Consumer research revealed the problem: the mix was too easy. Homemakers felt no sense of accomplishment, and the resulting cakes felt impersonal.

The solution was counterintuitive: make the product slightly harder to use. Betty Crocker reformulated the mix to require adding a fresh egg. The cakes were no better — arguably slightly less consistent. But sales surged. Adding the egg gave bakers a sense of involvement and ownership. They felt they had "made" the cake, and they valued it accordingly. Less convenience, more perceived value.

## When to use it

Apply IKEA Effect awareness whenever evaluating something you created or contributed to. Ask: "Do I think this is good because it's actually good, or because I made it?"

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is dismissing all ownership pride as bias. Taking pride in your work is motivating and healthy. The problem arises only when the pride prevents honest quality assessment — when you can't see flaws in your own creation because your effort investment has inflated your evaluation.

## Try it now

Think of something you created recently — a document, a plan, a design, a meal. Imagine a colleague had created the exact same thing. Would you evaluate it as highly? If there's a gap, the IKEA Effect is at work.
