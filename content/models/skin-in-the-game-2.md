## How it works

Accountability means designing systems where decision-makers face consequences for their decisions — both positive and negative. When consequences are present, decision quality improves. When consequences are absent, decisions optimise for the decision-maker's comfort, not the outcome.

This applies beyond finance: teachers who face accountability for student outcomes teach differently. Doctors who face consequences for misdiagnosis diagnose more carefully. Managers who share in their team's failures make different decisions than those who are insulated.

## Case study: How Singapore's government accountability system produced exceptional governance

Singapore's government operates on a principle of extreme accountability: ministers are paid competitive private-sector salaries (among the highest in the world for government officials), but they face genuine consequences for poor performance — public scrutiny, electoral defeat, and reputational damage in a small, transparent society.

The result: Singapore consistently ranks among the top nations for government effectiveness, low corruption, and quality of public services. The high pay attracts talent (removing the incentive for corruption as compensation). The accountability ensures that talent is deployed effectively. The system aligns incentives: decision-makers benefit from good governance and suffer from bad governance.

## When to use it

Apply accountability thinking when designing incentive structures. Ask: "Does the decision-maker bear meaningful consequences for bad outcomes?" If not, expect decisions optimised for the decision-maker's convenience rather than the organisation's success.

## Try it now

Think of a decision-maker in your organisation whose choices significantly affect others. What happens to them personally when those decisions go wrong? If the answer is "nothing," how could accountability be introduced?
