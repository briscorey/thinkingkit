## How it works

The halo effect, identified by Edward Thorndike, occurs when a positive impression in one area unconsciously influences your judgment in unrelated areas. An attractive person seems more intelligent. A confident speaker seems more knowledgeable. A successful entrepreneur's opinions on unrelated topics carry unearned weight.

The effect works in reverse too (the "horn effect"): a negative impression in one area biases perception of everything else. Someone who makes a bad first impression gets judged as less competent, even when their work quality is identical to someone who made a good first impression.

## Real-world examples

**Hiring.** Candidates who are tall, attractive, or well-dressed consistently receive higher competence ratings — even when interviewers are specifically trained to evaluate only job-relevant skills.

**Brand perception.** Apple's design reputation creates a halo that extends to perceptions of reliability, customer service, and even value for money — areas where they may or may not actually excel.

## When to use it

Watch for the halo effect whenever your impression of someone or something in one dimension seems to be colouring your judgment in others. Particularly dangerous in interviews, performance reviews, vendor selection, and any evaluation where you're supposed to assess specific attributes independently.

## Common mistakes

The mistake is thinking awareness eliminates the bias. It doesn't — the halo effect operates unconsciously even when you know about it. Structured evaluation criteria (scoring specific attributes independently, before forming an overall impression) are more effective than trying to "be objective."

## Try it now

Think of someone you admire. List three things you believe about them. For each belief, ask: "Do I have direct evidence for this, or am I projecting from my positive impression of them in a different area?"
