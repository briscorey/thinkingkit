## How it works

When something performs exceptionally well or badly, the next measurement is likely to be closer to the average. This isn't because of any corrective force — it's pure statistics. Extreme results require an unusual alignment of factors, and that alignment is unlikely to repeat.

This creates one of the most common reasoning errors: mistaking regression to the mean for a causal effect. A sports team has a terrible season and fires the coach. Next season, they improve. Was it the new coach? Maybe — but regression to the mean alone would predict improvement after an unusually bad year.

## Real-world examples

**"Sophomore slump."** An artist's debut album is exceptional. The second album is good but not as spectacular. Fans call it a disappointment. But the debut was an outlier — regression to the mean predicts a more average follow-up regardless of effort or talent.

**Medical treatment.** You feel terrible and take a remedy. You feel better. Was it the remedy? Possibly — but you also went to the doctor when your symptoms were at their worst (an extreme point). Regression to the mean alone would predict improvement.

## When to use it

Apply regression to the mean whenever you're evaluating the effect of an intervention that was introduced after an extreme result. Ask: "Would improvement (or decline) have happened anyway, simply because the previous result was extreme?"

## Common mistakes

The mistake is assuming regression to the mean explains everything. Genuine causal effects exist alongside statistical regression. The point is to consider regression as a baseline explanation before attributing change to whatever intervention happened to coincide with it.

## Try it now

Think of a time when something unusually bad happened, you made a change, and things improved. How much of the improvement could be explained by regression to the mean alone? What evidence would you need to confirm the change actually caused the improvement?
