## How it works

Scott Adams, the creator of Dilbert, distinguishes between goals and systems. A goal is a specific outcome you want to achieve: "lose 20 pounds," "write a book," "get promoted." A system is a process you follow regularly: "eat meals I've prepared myself," "write 500 words every morning," "learn one new skill each quarter."

Goals are binary — you've either achieved them or you haven't — which means you spend most of your time in a state of failure. Systems are ongoing — you either followed your system today or you didn't — which means you succeed every time you execute the process, regardless of the final outcome.

## Case study: How Jerry Seinfeld became the most successful comedian of his generation with one system

Jerry Seinfeld's writing system was famously simple: write jokes every single day and mark an X on a wall calendar for each day completed. His only rule: "Don't break the chain." The system wasn't about producing great material on any given day. It was about showing up consistently and letting volume produce quality over time.

Seinfeld didn't set a goal like "write the best comedy special ever." He built a system that made consistent output inevitable. Over years, the daily writing habit compounded into an enormous body of work, from which the best material could be selected. The chain — not any individual joke — was the product.

## When to use it

Apply systems thinking whenever you're setting goals. Ask: "What daily or weekly process, if followed consistently, would make this goal inevitable?" Then focus on building and maintaining the system rather than fixating on the goal.

## Common mistakes

The main mistake is abandoning goals entirely. Goals provide direction — they tell you which system to build. The right approach is to use goals for orientation and systems for execution.

## Try it now

Take your most important goal. What daily system, if followed for 90 days, would make meaningful progress inevitable? Write down the system in one sentence. Start tomorrow.
