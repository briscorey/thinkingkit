## Why teach mental models?

Mental models are the operating system of good thinking. Students who learn explicit thinking frameworks — rather than just content knowledge — develop transferable skills that apply across every subject and situation they'll encounter.

Teaching mental models builds metacognition (thinking about thinking), transfer (applying knowledge across domains), decision-making skills, and argument evaluation abilities. These are the skills that endure long after specific content knowledge fades.

## Getting started: Three approaches

**Approach 1: The Weekly Model.** Introduce one mental model per week. Spend 10 minutes explaining it, show two examples, and give students a prompt to apply it to something in their own lives. By the end of a semester, students have a toolkit of 15–20 frameworks.

**Approach 2: Embedded in existing content.** Rather than teaching models as a separate topic, name them when they appear naturally in your subject. "What we just saw in this science experiment is a feedback loop" or "The character in this novel fell for the sunk cost fallacy." Students learn to recognise models in context.

**Approach 3: The Thinking Routine.** Adapt Harvard Project Zero's visible thinking routines with mental model language. Before a discussion: "Which mental model applies here?" After a decision: "Let's run a pre-mortem on this plan." The routine makes thinking visible and habitual.

## Recommended models by age group

**Ages 10–13 (Middle School / MYP 1-3):**
Start with the most intuitive models that connect to students' daily experience. [Inversion](/models/inversion/) (ask what would make this fail), [Feedback Loops](/models/feedback-loops/) (vicious and virtuous cycles), [Sunk Cost Fallacy](/models/sunk-cost-fallacy/) (don't throw good money after bad), [Hanlon's Razor](/models/hanlons-razor/) (don't assume bad intent), and [Occam's Razor](/models/occams-razor/) (start with the simplest explanation).

**Ages 14–16 (High School / MYP 4-5):**
Add models that require more abstract thinking. [First Principles](/models/first-principles/), [Second-Order Thinking](/models/second-order-thinking/), [Confirmation Bias](/models/confirmation-bias/), [Probabilistic Thinking](/models/probabilistic-thinking/), and [Circle of Competence](/models/circle-of-competence/).

**Ages 17+ (Senior / DP):**
Students at this level can handle sophisticated models. [Bayesian Updating](/models/bayesian-updating/), [Antifragility](/models/antifragility/), [Ergodicity](/models/ergodicity/), [Goodhart's Law](/models/goodharts-law/), and [Wicked Problems](/models/wicked-problems/).

## Classroom activities

**The Model Match.** Present a real-world scenario (a news story, a historical event, a scientific discovery). Students work in pairs to identify which mental models apply and explain why. Groups share their analysis — different pairs often identify different models for the same scenario, which generates rich discussion.

**The Pre-Mortem Project.** Before students begin any significant project, run a class pre-mortem. "Imagine this project has failed. Why?" Students generate failure modes, then develop prevention plans. This teaches both the pre-mortem model and practical project management.

**The Bias Spot.** Give students the [Bias Check](/tools/bias-check/) tool and a controversial topic. They run through the 8 biases, identifying which ones might be affecting their own position. The goal isn't to change their minds — it's to make their thinking visible to themselves.

**The Decision Matrix Challenge.** For any group decision (choosing a project topic, planning an event, evaluating options), use the [Decision Matrix Builder](/tools/decision-matrix/). Students learn to make criteria explicit, weight them honestly, and score systematically — skills that transfer to every future decision.
